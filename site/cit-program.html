What CIT actually means

"CIT" stands for Crisis Intervention Team — a partnership between Inside Out and local law enforcement that changes who shows up when someone is in a mental health crisis. Instead of an officer arriving alone to a situation they may not have the training for, a CIT response sends a two-person team: a mental health professional from Inside Out and a CIT-trained officer who has completed a 40-hour course in mental health response and de-escalation. They arrive together, in plain clothes, in an unmarked car.

The reason it works is unhurried time. A standard 911 call moves fast — officers have other calls waiting and a narrow set of tools for how to end the encounter. A CIT response is different. The clinician and the officer can stay as long as it takes. There's no rush to make an arrest, no rush to transport, no rush to end the conversation. The first goal is to make sure the person in crisis is safe and heard. The next is to figure out, together, what they actually need — which is almost never jail.

CIT is not a magic wand and we won't pretend it is. It runs in specific jurisdictions, not everywhere in Iowa. Sometimes safety still requires hospital transport. Sometimes a situation is more complex than any single response can solve. But when CIT is the team that arrives, the encounter tends to look and feel very different from the one most people imagine when they imagine calling 911.

What it looks like when CIT arrives

No sirens, no flashing lights, no uniformed officer at the door alone. Here's what a CIT response looks like in practice.

A team of two, in plain clothes

A mental health professional and a CIT-trained officer arrive together, in an unmarked vehicle and ordinary clothing — so the moment doesn't draw stares from neighbors or escalate before anyone has spoken.

As much time as it takes

The team isn't racing to the next call. They can stay an hour. They can stay longer. The goal is a calmer, more grounded conversation — not a fast exit.

Listening before anything else

They introduce themselves by first name. They speak slowly. They ask what's happening and what would help. They are trained to de-escalate, not to take command.

A plan for what comes next

Once things have steadied, the team helps figure out the next step — staying home with a safety plan, a warm hand-off to ongoing care, or transport when it's truly needed. They follow up within a week.

How to help us help

If you're the person calling for help — for yourself or someone else — a few things make a CIT response more likely and more effective.

Say it's a mental health crisis

When you call 911, the very first thing to tell the dispatcher is that this is a mental health situation, not a crime. That single sentence helps route a CIT-trained response when one is available.

Share what's safe to share

Mention any diagnoses, medications, recent stressors, or weapons in the home. None of this is to label the person — it's to help the responders show up prepared and calm.

Lower the temperature where you can

Move pets, kids, or other people to another room if it's safe to do so. Turn down music. Keep your own voice low. The room a responder walks into shapes the first sixty seconds.

Stay nearby, stay steady

You don't have to manage the crisis. You do have to keep yourself okay enough to be present. Sit where the person can see you. Don't argue with what they're feeling. Help is coming.

How CIT works in your community

Three steps from making the call to a team at the door.

1

Call 911 — or our line if it's safer to talk first

If you or someone you love is in immediate danger, call 911 and tell the dispatcher this is a mental health crisis. If you'd rather start with a conversation, call our 24/7 line at 844-428-3878.

2

The dispatcher routes the call

In jurisdictions where Inside Out's CIT operates, the dispatcher can send a CIT response — a mental health professional and a CIT-trained officer together. Outside those areas, the responding officer may still be CIT-trained.

3

The team arrives — and stays

The clinician and officer arrive in an unmarked car, in plain clothes, and take the time to talk. Once things have steadied, they work with the person and family on what's next — and follow up within a week.

Where CIT runs today

Inside Out's Crisis Intervention Team currently partners with police departments in the Des Moines metro — including Clive, Urbandale, Waukee, and West Des Moines. In these jurisdictions, a CIT clinician is dispatched alongside a CIT-trained officer.

CIT-trained officers operate in additional partner agencies across the region, and we're working with our funding and training partners to expand co-responder coverage over time.

If your town isn't on this list:

Call 844-428-3878 anyway. We can talk you through your options — including Mobile Crisis Response, our crisis stabilization programs, and how to communicate with your local 911 dispatcher about a mental health call. You should not be alone in figuring this out.

If you're not sure whether to call 911, call us first.
