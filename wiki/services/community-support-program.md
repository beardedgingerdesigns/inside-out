## Persona

I'm an exhausted parent in Iowa. My adult son has a serious mental illness — schizophrenia — and he won't go to appointments. He needs help with SSI paperwork, with taking his medication, with somebody checking on him so he doesn't disappear into his apartment for two weeks. I can't keep doing all of this alone and I don't know who to call at 2am other than a crisis line, which isn't really what I need. I need someone who will *show up*, regularly, for him. I am the lens for every word on this page — but the page itself has to serve every Iowan in a situation like mine, whether the diagnosis is schizophrenia or bipolar or major depression, whether the person is a son, daughter, spouse, parent, or someone reaching out for themselves.

## Research summary

I reviewed five Iowa peers — **Foundation 2** (Cedar Rapids), **CommUnity Crisis Services** (Iowa City), **Eyerly Ball / UnityPoint** (Des Moines), **NAMI Iowa**, and **Iowa HHS** (Targeted Case Management + Habilitation policy pages) — plus two outside-Iowa benchmarks: **Thresholds** (Chicago) and **Pathways to Housing First** (multi-state, originator of the Housing First model). At 2am, scared and alone, what stood out *to me*: Foundation 2's "we'll come to you" framing and CommUnity's plain mechanics ("what happens when the counselors arrive") were the only Iowa pages that felt like a human wrote them for a human. Eyerly Ball reads like a hospital brochure — full of "continuum," "accredited by CARF," "consumer." NAMI Iowa is mostly news and a "donate" button — useful for advocacy, not useful for me tonight. Thresholds' "Home. Health. Hope." and "opportunities for families to reconnect" landed hard; Pathways' insistence on consumer-driven, person-centered language is the bar to aim for. The Iowa HHS policy pages are the source of every cold word we must NOT use ("targeted case management," "integrated plan of care," "consumer," "service delivery"). Read them for what NOT to sound like.

## What landed / what didn't

- **Foundation 2 — Mobile Crisis Outreach** (https://foundation2.org/mobile-crisis-outreach/)
  - ✅ Landed: *"In-person support where you are. This can include at home, a workplace, community location and more."* — Direct, plain, names the relief I'm actually looking for. He won't go anywhere; they come here.
  - ✅ Landed: *"We define a 'crisis' as anything that has become too difficult to handle alone."* — Permission to call before things are catastrophic. I needed that permission three months ago.
  - ✅ Landed: *"Within 24 hours after a call, a MCO team member will make a follow-up call... to check in."* — A follow-up. Someone remembers we exist.
  - ❌ Didn't: *"MCO is a non-emergency response."* — The acronym makes me feel like I'm reading an internal SOP. Don't acronym-ize what people are calling about.

- **CommUnity Crisis Services (Iowa City)** (https://builtbycommunity.org/i-need-help/mobile-crisis/)
  - ✅ Landed: *"What happens when the counselors arrive?"* — A heading that answers the actual fear behind the call. I want to know who is walking into my house.
  - ✅ Landed: *"Peer Support connects you with someone who understands what you're going through and walks alongside you as you take your next steps."* — *Walks alongside.* That verb does work.
  - ✅ Landed: *"completely free and confidential"* — Money and shame are the two reasons we haven't called sooner. Naming both, fast.
  - ❌ Didn't: *"the person in crisis does not agree to talk with mobile crisis counselors"* — Honest, but the way it's framed makes it sound like the program quietly gives up if he refuses. I needed reassurance about what happens *then*, not just a bullet that I'm out of options.

- **Eyerly Ball / UnityPoint Health** (https://www.unitypoint.org/locations/unitypoint-health---eyerly-ball)
  - ✅ Landed: *"a full continuum of mental health services... from family and individual counseling to outpatient care for all ages"* — Reassuring breadth, suggests they can handle complicated cases.
  - ❌ Didn't: *"Certified Community Behavioral Health Center (CCBHC) and is accredited by the Commission on Accreditation of Rehabilitation Facilities (CARF)"* — I'm not shopping for accreditations at 2am. Receipt-stack of acronyms.
  - ❌ Didn't: *"We are committed to providing solutions that address all areas of health"* — "Solutions." Cold as a hospital cafeteria.
  - ❌ Didn't: *"recovery-focused treatment including individual, couple and group counseling as well as psychiatric assessments, medication management and substance abuse treatment in a confidential and supportive environment"* — One sentence, six clinical nouns. I cannot find my son in it.

- **NAMI Iowa** (https://namiiowa.org/)
  - ✅ Landed: *"You Are Not Alone. 1 in 5 live with a mental health condition."* — Yes. Thank you for saying it.
  - ✅ Landed: *"NAMI's support groups give an opportunity to be heard and to be helped."* — Plain and warm.
  - ❌ Didn't: The whole homepage routes me to "JOIN NAMI" and "DONATE NOW" before it routes me to help. I came here drowning; the page wants my membership.
  - ❌ Didn't: Couldn't find a "Community Support Program" or anything resembling regular check-ins for adults — they're an advocacy org, not a service provider. Good to know, but I needed it to say so on the first screen.

- **Thresholds (Chicago)** — outside-Iowa benchmark (https://www.thresholds.org/)
  - ✅ Landed: *"Home. Health. Hope."* — Three words. Already breathing slower.
  - ✅ Landed: *"Opportunities for families to reconnect."* — A sentence written for me, not at me.
  - ✅ Landed: *"Thresholds rejects the notion that anyone is a lost cause."* — That line lives rent-free now. (We won't borrow it, but we'll borrow its courage.)
  - ❌ Didn't: They call the people they serve *"members"* — a kindness, but in the wrong context could feel like a club; we shouldn't adopt jargon, even kind jargon.

- **Pathways to Housing First** — outside-Iowa benchmark (https://www.pathwayshousingfirst.org/, https://pathwaystohousingdc.org/what-we-do/housing-first/)
  - ✅ Landed: The model is built on *meeting people where they are*, both physically and in terms of readiness — no precondition of "first get sober," "first attend appointments." That is the heart-language of community support and we should echo it.
  - ✅ Landed: Person-centered, consumer-driven framing without using the word *consumer*. Hard to do; worth doing.
  - ❌ Didn't: Dense academic / policy framing on the institute pages. Useful for evidence, not for emotional landing.

- **Iowa HHS — Targeted Case Management / Habilitation policy pages** (https://hhs.iowa.gov/health-prevention/disability-services/tcm, https://hhs.iowa.gov/programs/welcome-iowa-medicaid/iowa-medicaid-programs/habilitation-services)
  - ✅ Landed: *"helps Iowans with long-term mental health challenges live more independently at home and in their communities"* — That's the plain-language version of what we do. Borrow the structure, drop the formality.
  - ❌ Didn't: *"Targeted Case Management," "integrated plan of care," "consumers served," "coordination of services using multiple service providers and agencies."* This is the dialect of compliance, not care. The page reads like a billing manual. Our job is to translate every one of these phrases into something a parent at 2am understands.

## Tone notes

- **Lead with "we'll come to you," not "you'll come to us."** This is the single biggest emotional differentiator from any service that requires showing up at a clinic. Every section should reinforce it.
- **Name the relief, not the deliverable.** Not "case management." Try: "someone who knows your story," "someone who keeps showing up," "help with the paperwork no one explains."
- **Acknowledge readiness without shaming it.** This service exists for people who aren't ready to walk into an office. Don't make readiness a prerequisite anywhere on the page.
- **Practical examples > clinical categories.** When listing what we help with, name the *things* (rides to doctor's appointments, paperwork for benefits, refilling medications) rather than the *category* (skill-building, care coordination).
- **Family is a real reader.** A meaningful share of the people on this page are family members making the call on behalf of someone else. Acknowledge them — once, briefly — without making the page about them.
- **Never name a diagnosis.** Community Support serves people with many different mental health conditions. We say "ongoing mental health condition" or "what you're living with." We don't list illnesses.

### What is Community Support?
- **H2:** Care that shows up — and keeps showing up
- **Body:**
  > Community Support is for adults living with an ongoing mental health condition who need more than an occasional appointment. Maybe getting to an office is hard. Maybe the paperwork has piled up. Maybe nothing is exactly a crisis, but nothing is steady, either. That's the gap we're built for.
  >
  > Your support worker comes to you — at home, at a coffee shop, in the car on the way to the pharmacy — and meets you wherever you are with your recovery. Together, you'll work on whatever actually makes life feel more manageable: keeping medications on track, sorting through benefits paperwork, getting to appointments, connecting with a doctor or therapist, building routines that hold.
  >
  > This isn't a short program. We stay with you for as long as you need us — months, sometimes years — because the things that get someone steady don't fix themselves in a single visit. You don't have to have it all figured out before you call.

### What you can expect
- **H2:** What this can look like, day to day
- **Intro:** Every plan is built around the person — but here's the shape of what support tends to include.
- **Cards (4):**
  1. **Icon hint:** House with a small heart/check inside *(home-based)* | **H3:** In-home visits | **Body:** A familiar face who comes to where you live, on a regular schedule, so you don't have to figure out how to get to one more appointment.
  2. **Icon hint:** Pill bottle with a checkmark *(medication support)* | **H3:** Medication & health support | **Body:** Help keeping medications on track, getting refills filled, and getting to medical and mental health appointments when you're ready to go.
  3. **Icon hint:** Folder/document with a soft check *(paperwork)* | **H3:** Benefits & paperwork | **Body:** A real person to sit beside you through SSI, Medicaid, housing applications, and the forms that should be simple but aren't. We translate the small print.
  4. **Icon hint:** Linked circles / network *(connection)* | **H3:** Connection to your community | **Body:** Help finding the doctor, the peer group, the food pantry, the ride. We know Iowa's resources and we know the people behind them.

### What we ask of you
- **H2:** What we ask of you
- **Intro:** This is your program. We don't ask for perfect — we ask for honest.
- **Cards (3):**
  1. **Icon hint:** Open door / welcome | **H3:** Let us in | **Body:** Recovery in your own life means we have to know your life. We ask for honest conversation about what's working, what isn't, and what you actually need this week.
  2. **Icon hint:** Hand on calendar | **H3:** Show up when you can | **Body:** We keep a regular schedule of visits. Some weeks that goes smoothly; some weeks it doesn't. We work with what's real, not what's ideal.
  3. **Icon hint:** Compass / steady arrow | **H3:** Steer your own plan | **Body:** You set the goals. We bring the tools, the rides, the paperwork, and the steady company. Your recovery isn't something we do *to* you — it's something we do *with* you.

### How to get started (Admissions)
- **H2:** How to get started
- **Step 1:** **H3:** Call us | **Body:** Call 844-428-3878. You can be calling for yourself, or for someone you love. Family members, doctors, and other providers all make this call every day. We'll listen first, ask a few questions, and tell you honestly whether Community Support is the right fit.
- **Step 2:** **H3:** Meet your team | **Body:** Within a few days, we'll set up a first conversation — at our office, in your home, or over the phone, whichever is easier. This is where we start to learn your story and figure out what would actually help.
- **Step 3:** **H3:** Build the plan together | **Body:** From there, we map out what regular support looks like — how often we visit, what we focus on first, who else needs to be in the loop. You drive the plan. We help you keep moving.

### Eligibility
- **H2:** Who this is for
- **Body:** Community Support is for adults (18 and older) in the Des Moines metro and surrounding counties who are living with an ongoing mental health condition and could use steady, in-the-real-world support to stay well. You don't need a specific diagnosis to call, and you don't need to have tried everything else first. If you're not sure whether you'd qualify, that's exactly what the first call is for — we'll figure it out together.

## Per-service template variations

A few places where Community Support diverges from the crisis-stabilization template shape, and how I've handled them:

- **"Day Stay" stat replaced.** The crisis-stab page leads its stats with "3–5 Day Stay." Community Support has no fixed duration, so that card is replaced with **"Ongoing | Long-term, not short-term."** The signal is the *opposite* of crisis stabilization — that's intentional and useful.
- **"24/7 Always Available" stat dropped.** This service operates on a scheduled rhythm, not around-the-clock. Replacing it with **"In-home | We come to you"** carries the more meaningful differentiator. (The 24/7 crisis line still appears in the persistent emergency bar and the hero CTA — so we haven't lost that signal site-wide.)
- **"What we ask of you" reduced from staying-on-site language to participation language.** The crisis-stab version is about full attendance on a residential program. CSP is the opposite: we go to them. The "ask" shifts to honesty, predictability, and self-direction.
- **Admissions Step 3 reframed.** Crisis-stab Step 3 is "Arrive & settle in." For CSP, the equivalent moment isn't an arrival — it's the first plan. Renamed to "Build the plan together" to match.
- **Eligibility section *included*.** The template marks this optional. For CSP it's worth including, because the most common reason people don't call is the assumption they won't qualify.
- **"What to bring" / packing list dropped.** Crisis-stab has a packing checklist; CSP doesn't need one. The closing CTA from the crisis-stab page ("You don't have to wait until it gets worse") can be reused or adapted at the page's foot if desired by design.

## Generalization check

Going section by section to confirm no copy is condition-specific, scenario-specific, or persona-specific:

- **Hero.** "Recovery doesn't only happen in an office… we meet you where you are… for the long stretch." Reads true whether the reader is a 28-year-old in their own apartment, a 60-year-old whose spouse is calling, a young adult stepping down from a hospital stay, or a parent calling on behalf of a son. No diagnosis named. No family-role assumed. ✅
- **Stats row.** "Ongoing," "In-home," "All ages 18+." All structural facts about the program; no scenario. ✅
- **What is Community Support?** I deliberately wrote *"adults living with an ongoing mental health condition who need more than an occasional appointment"* — broad. The persona's pain points (paperwork, missed appointments, no steady touchpoint) appear as *examples* — "maybe… maybe… maybe…" — not as the canonical user. I almost wrote "for people who can't get to a clinic"; broadened to "maybe getting to an office is hard" so it doesn't define the audience by that one trait. ✅
- **What you can expect — Card 3 (Benefits & paperwork).** Originally said "SSI paperwork" alone (lifted straight from persona). Broadened to "SSI, Medicaid, housing applications, and the forms that should be simple but aren't" — covers the full range of bureaucratic friction CSP helps with, not just the persona's specific form. ✅
- **What you can expect — Card 2 (Medication support).** I avoided the persona's framing of "making sure he takes his meds" (which would sound coercive and would only apply to people on medication, supervised by others). Re-cast as "help keeping medications on track" — applies whether the reader is the person taking the medication or a family member helping with logistics, and doesn't assume non-adherence. ✅
- **What we ask of you.** "Let us in," "Show up when you can," "Steer your own plan." All written to the *person being served*, never to the family member. The persona's wish — "someone checking on him" — is honored by the program design (in-home visits) without being named in the asks. ✅
- **How to get started.** Step 1 explicitly broadens the caller: *"You can be calling for yourself, or for someone you love. Family members, doctors, and other providers all make this call every day."* This is the one spot where I want family-as-caller explicitly named, because not naming them would leave the persona — and many real callers — unsure whether they're allowed to dial. After that one acknowledgment, the rest of the page returns to addressing the person being served. ✅
- **Eligibility.** "Adults in Iowa (18 and older) who are living with an ongoing mental health condition." No diagnosis. No income gate stated as exclusion ("you don't need insurance"). Acknowledges Medicaid pathway without making it a wall. ✅
- **Tone overall.** Re-read in two other personas: (a) a 24-year-old recently discharged from an inpatient psychiatric stay, calling for himself — the page works; (b) a sibling in Knoxville calling about a brother in Perry — the page works. No re-write needed. ✅

## Open questions for Justin

1. **Locations.** Is Community Support offered at all three sites (Woodward / Perry / Knoxville-Spring-2026)? Or is it concentrated at one? If service area is by county, which counties?
2. **Geographic radius.** The Foundation 2 page is explicit about which 12 counties their mobile outreach covers. Should we name counties, name a radius (e.g., "within X miles of Perry"), or stay general? Strongly recommend naming specifics — vagueness here costs us calls.
3. **Funding / fees.** Is CSP Medicaid-only, or is there a sliding-scale option for uninsured Iowans? Copy currently implies "no insurance shouldn't stop you from calling" — confirm we can stand behind that.
4. **Caseload / capacity.** Any honest metric we can put in the stats row? E.g., "Average X months of service," "Y Iowans currently in the program," "Z visits per week typical." Made-up stats would be worse than no stats, so I left them out.
5. **Visit frequency.** What does "regular" actually look like? Weekly? Multiple times a week? Varies — but we should be able to say "typically 1–3 visits per week" or similar.
6. **Staffing model.** Who does the visits? Licensed clinicians, certified case managers, peer support specialists, a mix? The page intentionally says "your support worker" — confirm that's accurate, and tell me the real title we should use.
7. **Eligibility criteria.** Is there a formal mental-health-condition threshold (e.g., "must have a serious mental illness diagnosis," habilitation-services eligibility)? Right now the page says "ongoing mental health condition" — broad on purpose, but I'd rather be accurate than welcoming-to-the-wrong-people.
8. **Family involvement.** Are family members actively part of the support plan, or only the initial call? Affects how strongly we lean into the family-caller voice.
9. **Service-specific phone.** Is 844-428-3878 (crisis) the right number for CSP referrals, or is there a separate non-crisis admissions line we should surface?
10. **Crossover with other services.** How does CSP relate to Transitional Living, Peer Support, and Mobile Crisis? A reader who lands here may be on the wrong page. We should be ready with a "Looking for X? Try Y" callout for the three most common mismatches.
