## Persona

It is 11pm. I am alone in the kitchen of a farmhouse 40 minutes from the nearest hospital. My husband is on the couch, decompensating, refusing to move, refusing to talk to me, refusing to get in the car. I cannot drag him out. I cannot call 911 — the last time a deputy came out for a "welfare check" two counties over, it ended with a man I knew face-down on his own porch. I don't want police. I don't want lights and sirens. I want a person — a real person — to walk through my back door, sit down on the couch next to him, and help me figure out what comes next. I don't have the patience for a form. I don't have time to be transferred. I need to know, in the first three seconds of looking at the screen, that the people I'm about to call will come *here*.

## Research summary

Reviewed five Iowa-anchored mobile crisis programs (Foundation 2 / Cedar Rapids, CommUnity Crisis Services / Iowa City, Eyerly Ball / Des Moines metro + 18 surrounding counties, CICS Mobile Response, Broadlawns via 911 for Polk County) and two national benchmarks (CAHOOTS / Eugene, OR — the oldest mobile crisis team in the U.S.; Denver STAR — the most-cited modern alternative-response model). Cross-referenced against Iowa HHS's statewide framework and the Crisis Now policy exemplars. *To the persona*, the single biggest "this is the one" moment is any page that — above the fold, in plain English — promises that a trained mental health team will come to where she is, that it is not the police, that it does not cost money, and that she does not have to convince her husband to leave first. The Iowa programs that nail this fastest are CommUnity ("Completely Free and Confidential") and Foundation 2 ("in-person support where you are"). The programs that almost lose her are the county-government pages that lead with acronyms ("MCRT," "CICS," "BHDS") or that read like a service-eligibility worksheet.

## What landed / what didn't

- **Foundation 2 Crisis Services** (https://foundation2.org/mobile-crisis-outreach/)
  - Landed: "in-person support where you are, including at home, a workplace, community location and more" — the persona reads "at home" and exhales. The page does the work of saying *we don't expect you to move him*.
  - Landed: "Counselors will respond within one hour of receiving the request" — concrete number, not a vague "as soon as possible." She can plan.
  - Didn't: "Mobile Crisis Outreach is intended for situations where clients would benefit more from a face-to-face meeting than from a call to the Foundation 2 crisis hotline." Persona reads this as a gatekeeping sentence — "are we *eligible*? are we using the *right* service?" — at the exact moment she has no capacity for that question.
  - Didn't: Acronym "MCO" used in body copy. She does not care what it stands for.

- **CommUnity Crisis Services** (https://builtbycommunity.org/i-need-help/mobile-crisis/)
  - Landed: Page title and meta: "Mobile Crisis Response | Completely Free and Confidential." Three words she needs to see immediately are "free" and "confidential." Money and shame are two of the top reasons she hasn't called yet.
  - Landed: "Mobile Crisis Outreach is a 24/7 program that dispatches mental health counselors to homes, schools, ER's or public places." The list of locations is doing real reassurance work — she sees "homes" and knows this isn't a clinic-only service dressed up in mobile language.
  - Landed (from their FAQ): The fact that responders arrive in "unmarked vehicles." This is the single most important sentence on any mobile crisis page for someone afraid of escalation. Inside Out should consider an equivalent.
  - Didn't: Use of the word "dispatch" — borderline. Persona reads "dispatch" as ambulance/police language. The team comes *to* her; they don't get *deployed.*

- **Eyerly Ball / UnityPoint** (https://search.ne211.org/search/fd7c709d-4e96-554f-8cda-d29e4273d024)
  - Landed: "anyone within the 18-county region may call" + an explicit list of counties. She lives in a rural place and needs to know *before clicking anything else* whether her county is covered.
  - Didn't: "averts unnecessary visits to hospital emergency rooms and subsequent hospitalizations or incarcerations." Clinically true; emotionally tone-deaf. "Incarceration" is not a word she wants to read while looking for help. Cuts.
  - Didn't: "an active part of a state-wide crisis system of prevention, response and postvention services." Word salad. "Postvention" is a clinical term no caller will Google.

- **Your Life Iowa / Iowa HHS** (https://yourlifeiowa.org/get-help/crisis-services)
  - Landed: "Trained crisis counselors come to you and provide in-person care." Simple. Direct. The page she would want to land on.
  - Landed: "In most situations, crisis staff arrive within 60 minutes." Specific, honest, manages expectations.
  - Landed: Acknowledgement that Mobile Crisis is *voluntary* — the person in crisis has the right to refuse to speak with counselors. For a caller whose loved one is refusing help, this is actually a relief, not a deterrent: it means the team will still come and talk to *her*.
  - Didn't: Forces caller through Your Life Iowa as a gateway. Persona doesn't want to call a hotline that then dispatches another team. She wants one number.

- **CAHOOTS / White Bird Clinic, Eugene, OR** (https://whitebirdclinic.org/what-is-cahoots/)
  - Landed: 30+ years of operating language refined down to: "trauma-informed de-escalation and harm reduction." Concept the persona feels even if she doesn't use those words: *this is not going to be a confrontation.*
  - Landed: Public stats — "in 2019, CAHOOTS teams responded to almost 24,000 calls and requested police back-up only 250 times." That ratio (~1%) is the most reassuring single data point in mobile crisis. If Inside Out has any comparable number, it should appear on the page.
  - Didn't: The page is built for policymakers and reporters, not callers. Hero is mission-statement language. Reads as institutional. Persona bounces if she lands here in crisis.

- **Denver STAR / WellPower** (https://www.wellpower.org/star-program/)
  - Landed: "the team — dressed in street clothes — provides direct clinical de-escalation." "Street clothes" is the visual companion to "unmarked vehicles." Both quietly say: *no one outside will know.*
  - Didn't: STAR is a 911-rerouted program; their page assumes the reader is a policymaker or a curious citizen, not someone trying to *summon* the team. Inside Out's page has to do something STAR's doesn't: actually convert a caller in distress.

- **Crisis Now** (https://crisisnow.com/mobile-crisis/)
  - Landed: Frames mobile crisis as one tier of a three-part system (line → mobile → stabilization). Useful conceptually for our partner-provider audience.
  - Didn't: Pure policy-document register. Not for the persona.

## Tone notes

- Lead with the promise, then the practical details. The first 30 words must say: we come to you, we are not the police, it is free, the line is open right now.
- Use plain second person throughout. "You can call. We come to you. You don't have to convince anyone of anything before the call."
- Acknowledge — without belaboring — the fear that calling for help will make things worse. Name who arrives: a mental health team. Name how they arrive: in plain clothes, in an unmarked car (pending confirmation from Justin — see open questions).
- Iowa-specific without being rural-only. The persona is rural; the *service* is not. Avoid framing that excludes someone calling from inside Des Moines.
- Drop every clinical word that has a plain-English version: no "dispatch," "deploy," "intervention" (as a noun), "intake," "postvention," "MCRT."
- Where the previous template said "your stay," there is no stay. Where it said "admissions," there is no admission. Re-read every word for stay/facility assumptions before publishing.

- **H1:** When you can't get to help, help can come to you.
- **Sub:** Mobile Crisis Response sends a trained mental health team to wherever the crisis is — your home, a friend's living room, a parking lot, a school. No hospital trip required, no convincing anyone to get in a car. Free, confidential, and answering right now at 844-428-3878.

### What is Mobile Crisis Response?
- **H2:** A team that comes to the crisis — not the other way around.
- **Body:**
  - **P1:** Mobile Crisis Response is exactly what it sounds like: when someone is in a mental health crisis and getting them to a hospital, clinic, or office isn't possible — or isn't the right move — we send a small team of trained mental health professionals to meet them where they are. It might be your living room. It might be a relative's house. It might be a school office or the side of a road. Wherever the crisis is, that's where we go.
  - **P2:** We are not law enforcement. The team that arrives is made up of mental health workers — counselors, social workers, and peer specialists who have been through their own hard things and come out the other side. They arrive quietly, in plain clothes, and their job is to listen, help everyone breathe, and figure out the next right step together with you.
  - **P3:** There is no cost to call, no insurance question to answer first, and nothing you have to prove to qualify. If you are in Iowa and someone needs help, the line is open at **844-428-3878** — 24 hours a day, every day of the year.

### What you can expect (when our team arrives)
- **H2:** What it looks like when we get there
- **Intro:** Calling for help is hard. Here is exactly what happens after you do.
- **Cards (4):**
  - **Icon: door/knock | H3: A quiet arrival**
    Two team members, plain clothes, an unmarked vehicle. No lights. No uniforms. No one driving by your house will know we are there.
  - **Icon: chair/conversation | H3: Time to talk**
    We sit down. We listen — to the person in crisis, to whoever called us, to whoever else is in the room. There is no rush, and no required script. The goal is to lower the temperature, not to take over.
  - **Icon: compass/map | H3: A plan you help build**
    Together we look at what's actually happening and what could help next. That might mean a safety plan for the night, a referral to ongoing care, or a stay in our crisis stabilization program. Staying home, safely, is often the right answer.
  - **Icon: phone-callback | H3: A follow-up call**
    Within 24 hours of our visit, someone from our team checks in by phone — to see how the night went, answer questions, and connect you to anything you still need.

### What we ask of you
- **H2:** What we ask from you
- **Intro:** Almost nothing. But these three things help us help you.
- **Cards (3):**
  - **Icon: phone | H3: Make the call, even if you're not sure**
    You do not have to know what kind of crisis it is. You do not have to convince the person in crisis to agree first. If you are worried, that is reason enough to dial.
  - **Icon: shield | H3: Tell us if there's a weapon**
    Our team is unarmed. If there is a gun, knife, or other weapon in the home, please tell the person who answers the phone. It changes nothing about whether we come — it only changes how.
  - **Icon: location-pin | H3: Help us find the door**
    A driveway, a gate code, a back entrance, a dog — anything you can tell us upfront gets us to you faster. In rural Iowa, the address alone is sometimes not enough.

### What happens when you call (replaces "Admissions")
- **H2:** What happens when you call
- **Step 1 — H3: You reach a real person, right away**
  **Body:** Dial **844-428-3878**. The phone is answered 24 hours a day by a trained crisis counselor — not a voicemail, not an automated tree. They will ask a few questions: where you are, who is with you, what is happening. Take your time. They have time.
- **Step 2 — H3: We decide together what kind of help fits**
  **Body:** For some calls, talking on the phone is enough — the counselor will stay on the line as long as you need. For others, sending the team is the right move. You can ask for the team directly; you don't have to wait for us to offer.
- **Step 3 — H3: The team comes to you**
  **Body:** Once the team is on the way, the counselor on the phone will stay with you until they arrive — or until you tell them you're okay. When the team gets there, the work continues with them, in person, at whatever pace the moment allows.

### Eligibility & Coverage
- **H2:** Who this is for, and where we serve
- **Body:** Anyone of any age in any kind of mental or emotional crisis. You can call for yourself, for someone you love, for a student, for a patient, for a neighbor. You do not need a diagnosis, and you do not need to have tried anything else first. If you are not sure whether this is "really" a crisis, that uncertainty is reason enough to call — we will help you figure it out together.

- **Where our team responds (placeholder per Justin's 2026-05-22 directive — confirm precise list):** Mobile Crisis Response currently covers the Des Moines metro and surrounding counties — **Polk, Dallas, Warren, Madison, and neighboring counties.** If you're calling from outside this area, dial 844-428-3878 anyway — we will help you find the right team near you.

## Per-service template variations

This service requires several content-shape adjustments from the crisis-stabilization template. Flagging for the build phase:

- **"Admissions" → "What happens when you call."** No physical admission happens; the team comes to the caller.
- **"What to expect during your stay" → "What it looks like when we get there."** No stay. The encounter happens at the caller's location.
- **"What to bring" → removed.** There is nothing to bring. (Replace this slot, if the build phase wants symmetry, with the existing "What we ask of you" card grid.)
- **"How to get started" 3-step graphic → kept, with steps re-mapped to: call → assess by phone → team arrives.**

For Mobile Crisis, a hero that shows two team members walking up a porch, or a phone in a hand at a kitchen table, would do more emotional work than a clinical interior would.

## Generalization check

Re-reading each section against the persona-as-lens rule:

- **Hero** — "wherever the crisis is — your home, a friend's living room, a parking lot, a school" passes the test. The persona's exact scenario is implicit but not named. A self-caller, a school counselor, or an ER discharge planner all land on this language without friction.
- **What is Mobile Crisis Response?** — "if you are in Iowa and someone needs help" reads cleanly whether "someone" is you, your spouse, your student, your patient. No fix needed.
- **What you can expect** — "two team members, plain clothes, an unmarked vehicle. No lights. No uniforms" reassures the persona but works equally for an ER discharge planner explaining the service to a patient who is scared of police. **Pass.**
- **What we ask of you** — Card 1 "you do not have to convince the person in crisis to agree first" was originally drafted from the persona's scenario. Re-read: still rings true for a teacher calling about a student, a sibling calling about a brother, or a patient calling about themselves (in which case "the person in crisis" is themselves, and they don't have to convince themselves of anything either). **Pass, but watch this on copy edit.**
- **What happens when you call** — Step 1 "where you are, who is with you, what is happening" works for any caller type. **Pass.**
- **Eligibility** — Explicitly enumerates self-callers, family, providers, schools, ER staff, neighbors. **Pass, and this section is doing important load-bearing generalization work.**

Near-misses broadened. Final language is service-wide, not persona-specific.

## Partner-provider perspective

Referring partners (law enforcement, ER discharge planners, school counselors, primary care, judges of mental-health-court diversion) read this page very differently from a family caller. They are not making one emotional decision; they are evaluating whether this service is a reliable handoff for a population they are responsible for. What earns their trust:

1. **Predictable response time.** A specific number ("within 60 minutes" or whatever Inside Out can commit to) is more valuable to a provider than empathetic prose. Providers won't refer to a service that might or might not show up.
2. **Coverage area, explicit.** A clearly listed set of counties or a coverage map. Providers triage on geography — they will not refer if they're guessing.
3. **Team composition.** Who actually arrives — credentials, training hours, mix of disciplines (LMSW, peer specialist, RN, etc.). Providers want to know if our team can handle co-occurring substance use, child cases, dementia, etc.
4. **Handoff clarity.** What happens *after* the team arrives — do they transport, do they coordinate with the referring provider, do they send a written follow-up note? ER discharge planners specifically need to know how to close their loop.
5. **What we don't do.** The page should be honest about the line: we are not EMS, we are not law enforcement, we do not respond to active violence or armed standoffs. Naming this earns trust faster than pretending to do everything.
6. **One referral number, same as the public.** Providers respect simplicity. 844-428-3878 should be both the public line and the partner line (with optional provider-only intake form behind partner-providers.html for non-urgent referrals).
7. **Language that respects their role.** No "ditch the cops" or "instead of 911" framing. Many of our providers *are* police, ER, and 911 dispatchers. The framing is "Mobile Crisis Response is a tool you can hand off to," not "Mobile Crisis Response is what 911 should have been."

**Provider callout bar copy (suggested seed, for build phase to refine):**
> Referring a patient, student, or community member? Mobile Crisis Response accepts referrals from law enforcement, ER staff, schools, primary care, and community providers. Same number as the public line — 844-428-3878 — or use the Provider Hub for non-urgent referrals.

## Open questions for Justin

1. **Coverage area.** Which Iowa counties does Inside Out's Mobile Crisis Response cover today? Will Knoxville's Spring 2026 opening expand the radius? The page needs an honest answer here before launch.
2. **Response time target.** Can we commit to "within 60 minutes" (statewide Iowa standard), or do we want to underpromise ("within 90 minutes" or "as fast as roads allow") and overdeliver?
3. **Team composition.** Who shows up — LMSW + peer? RN included? Two-person minimum? This drives the "team that comes" card and the partner-provider trust block.
4. **Hours.** True 24/7? Or daytime-only with an after-hours referral to 988? Be precise; rural callers will time-test this claim by accident at 3am.
5. **Plain clothes / unmarked vehicle confirmation.** This is a high-stakes promise (it directly addresses fear of escalation). Confirm we actually operate this way before we publish it.
6. **What happens outside the coverage area?** If a caller from a county we don't serve dials 844-428-3878, what does the counselor do? Warm handoff to 988, to a neighboring provider, to law enforcement? The page should hint at this without being defeatist.
7. **CIT relationship.** Mobile Crisis Response and the CIT Program (co-responder with law enforcement) are adjacent services that will confuse callers. We need a one-sentence "how this differs from CIT" — likely something like "CIT pairs our team with law enforcement on 911 calls; Mobile Crisis Response is the team alone, called directly by you."
8. **Voluntary nature.** Iowa HHS and Your Life Iowa both highlight that Mobile Crisis is voluntary — the person in crisis can refuse to talk. Do we want to surface that on the public page (it can reassure both the caller and the loved one) or leave it as something the counselor explains by phone?
9. **Cost confirmation.** Most Iowa peers list this service as free. Confirm.
10. **Follow-up call timing.** Statewide pattern is "within 24 hours." Confirm Inside Out matches; if not, adjust the card.
