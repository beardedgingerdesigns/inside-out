## Persona

I'm 31. I just got discharged from a five-day inpatient stay — the second one this year — and the hospital social worker is asking where I'll go. The honest answer is *I don't know.* My apartment is gone. My mom's couch isn't an option anymore; we've burned through it. I'm not in active crisis right now, but I'm not anywhere near being able to sign a lease, hold a job, and manage everything on my own either. I'm searching for a place that isn't a shelter and isn't a hospital — somewhere I can land for a stretch, get my footing, have people around, and not have to figure out the rest of my life in the next 72 hours. My biggest fears about a place like that: that I'll be treated like a patient instead of a person, that they'll lock the doors, that I'll get kicked out for the kind of stuff that's actually normal for someone in my situation, and — most of all — that nobody can tell me how long I get to stay. I am the lens for this page, but it has to also work for: a man being released from the county jail with nowhere to go; an adult coming out of a 90-day residential treatment stay; someone who just lost their housing and can't safely live alone; a family member ("my daughter just got out of the hospital — where does she go?") trying to find an answer at 11pm.

## Research summary

I reviewed four Iowa peers — **Foundation 2** (Cedar Rapids, the state's flagship crisis nonprofit), **CommUnity Crisis Services** (Iowa City, runs the *Healing Prairie Farm* program), **UnityPoint Health – Eyerly Ball** (Polk County CCBHC, operates *Cummins House* and *Francis House* residential care facilities plus habilitation homes), and **Optimae LifeServices** (statewide Iowa health-and-human-services org with residential care across 50+ counties) — plus **Iowa HHS** for residential-care/habilitation vocabulary, and two outside-Iowa benchmarks: **Gould Farm** (Massachusetts therapeutic community, the country's oldest residential program for people recovering from mental illness) and **Fountain House** (NYC clubhouse model, the global gold standard for community-based recovery). I also re-checked **Pathways Housing First** since their philosophy is the right backstop for any residential service we describe.

To the persona, the standouts:
- **Eyerly Ball's Cummins/Francis House framing** ("15 beds… 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. Activities of daily living and skills training are provided to teach residents how to live independently. Skills training includes cooking, budgeting, hygiene, cleaning, social skills, community resources and medication management") is the most informationally honest Iowa peer — but it reads like a regulatory disclosure, not a place a human being would choose to live. The list of skills is right; the voice is wrong.
- **Optimae's tagline** — *"At your side. On your side."* — is the closest any Iowa peer comes to writing the way I want this page to sound. Their motto does in five words what most Iowa peers can't do in five paragraphs.
- **Foundation 2 does not offer transitional living.** This matters: the state's most-googled crisis nonprofit ends at the crisis line and mobile outreach. There is no obvious step-down. That's the gap Inside Out is filling. Naming it isn't necessary on the page, but understanding it shapes the urgency of the page.
- **CommUnity's *Healing Prairie Farm*** is interesting — a farm-based mental health program that's clearly more than crisis response. Their language is sparse on their public page but the existence of the program tells me Iowans are looking for residential-adjacent options that aren't a hospital.
- **Gould Farm and Fountain House** are the tonal targets, in two different directions: Gould Farm for the *"meaningful work, real relationships, time"* dignity-of-recovery register; Fountain House for the *"purpose, belonging, and dignity"* language.
- **The single biggest mistake** I see across these pages: nobody tells the reader, in the first 30 seconds, how long they get to stay. Length-of-stay is the question this service answers and most peers bury it or omit it entirely. Our page has to lead with it.

## Tone notes

- **Answer the duration question in the first screen.** "How long can I stay?" is the single most-asked question this page faces. Every other Iowa peer ducks it. We won't. Lead with the honest range — even if it's a band, e.g. *"most stays are X to Y."* If we can't yet land on a number, flag it in Open Questions and write copy that holds the slot. **Vagueness is the enemy.**
- **Use the word *home,* not *facility.*** "Facility" is what a hospital social worker writes on a form. "Home" is what a person lives in. The whole page should read like the house has a kitchen with the lights on, not a fluorescent dayroom.
- **Name the dignity move out loud.** "You're a person here, not a patient." Don't be coy about it; readers are bracing for the opposite.
- **Don't make the doors sound locked.** The persona's biggest fear (and family-caller's biggest fear) is that this is a soft lockdown. Tell them: this is voluntary; you can leave; visitors are welcome; you have a key. Use those exact words if at all possible.
- **Name a daily rhythm without sounding scheduled-to-death.** Recovery happens in routine. But too much "morning group, 11am chore, 1pm meeting, 3pm reflection" reads like a treatment center, not a place to live. Give the shape, not the timetable.
- **Treat *next step* as the goal, not *graduation.*** Avoid "discharge," "completion," "graduation." Use "moving on," "next chapter," "your own place."
- **Never name a diagnosis.** "What you're living with," "an ongoing mental health condition," "what brought you here." Not bipolar, not schizophrenia, not depression. (Per Justin's 2026-05-22 directive.)
- **Acknowledge the people sending the reader here.** A real share of the people landing on this page were sent by a hospital social worker, a probation officer, a homeless services case manager, or a family member. They didn't necessarily choose to look. The page should make showing up here feel like an active choice, not a transfer.
- **Don't claim to be a treatment program.** This is a *step between* treatment and independence — not treatment itself. Outpatient therapy, medication management, psychiatric care all happen *elsewhere*, with our support to stay connected. Being clear about what we are and aren't keeps the reader from showing up with the wrong expectations.

### What is Transitional Living?

- **H2:** A place to live while you figure out the next place to live
- **Body:**

  > Transitional Living is a residential home for adults who are stepping out of something intense — a hospital stay, a stabilization program, a long stretch in jail, a period without safe housing — and aren't quite ready to land in a place of their own. You'll live in a shared home on our Woodward campus, with your own bed, support staff in the house around the clock, and a real routine to put your feet under you.
  >
  > This is not a hospital. It's not a lockdown. It's a home you choose to be in, with people who choose to be there too. The doors aren't locked. You have a key. You can leave the house, see your family, hold a job, take a walk. What we ask is that you're choosing to be here — and that you're willing to do the work that getting steady takes.
  >
  > Stays are measured in weeks and months — long enough to actually rebuild, not so long that you forget how to be on your own. The end of your time with us isn't a discharge; it's a move. Into your own apartment, back to family, into supported housing, wherever steady looks like for you next.

### What you can expect

- **H2:** What your days here actually look like
- **Intro:** Days have shape without being scripted. Mornings tend to be routine and quiet; afternoons are when most of the work happens; evenings belong to the house. Here's the shape.
- **Cards (4):**

  1. **Icon hint:** Sun coming up / coffee cup *(morning rhythm)*
     **H3:** A morning you can count on
     **Body:** Wake up in your own room, in a house that's warm and not on a hospital schedule. Breakfast together if you want it. Medications taken with someone there to help if that's part of your plan. The day doesn't start with an alarm and a clipboard.

  2. **Icon hint:** Notebook + pen *(planning, goals)*
     **H3:** A plan that's yours
     **Body:** Once a week, you sit down with your case manager and shape what the week ahead looks like — appointments, goals, the practical pieces (benefits, ID, transportation, housing leads). You set the direction; we bring the steady company and the know-how.

  3. **Icon hint:** Toolbox / hands learning *(skills practice)*
     **H3:** Real skills, practiced in a real home
     **Body:** Cooking. Budgeting. Laundry. Getting yourself to an appointment. Sorting through the mail. The everyday skills that hold a life together — practiced in a kitchen, not a classroom, with the kind of patient help most of us never got the first time around.

  4. **Icon hint:** Two figures together / shared meal *(community)*
     **H3:** People around when you want them
     **Body:** Shared meals, quiet evenings, the kind of low-key company that makes a house feel like a home. You can keep to yourself when you need to. You can sit at the table when you don't. Both are fine here.

### What we ask of you

- **H2:** What we ask of you
- **Intro:** Transitional Living works because everyone in the house is choosing to be here. The asks are real, but they're not a gauntlet.
- **Cards (3):**

  1. **Icon hint:** Open door / handshake
     **H3:** Choose to be here
     **Body:** This is a voluntary program. You're not being placed; you're moving in. We ask that you show up because you've decided to, even on the days that's hard — and that you tell us honestly when it stops being the right fit.

  2. **Icon hint:** House + heart *(care of place)*
     **H3:** Help keep the home what it is
     **Body:** A shared home runs on small daily things — keeping the kitchen workable, respecting your housemates, keeping the home substance-free, telling us when something's off before it becomes a bigger thing. The house holds us when we hold it.

  3. **Icon hint:** Stepping forward *(active engagement)*
     **H3:** Keep moving toward your next place
     **Body:** Engage in your outpatient care, work on the things in your plan, take real steps toward what comes after this — a job, school, volunteer work, your own housing, reconnecting with people. Progress, not perfection.

### How to get started (Admissions)

- **H2:** How to apply
- **Intro:** Most people come to us through a referral from a hospital, a stabilization program, a probation officer, or another provider — but you can also reach out yourself, or a family member can call on your behalf. Here's the path in.

- **Step 1:** **H3:** Reach out
  **Body:** Call 844-428-3878. A real person will answer. They'll ask a few questions about what's going on, what you're stepping out of, and what would help — and they'll tell you honestly whether Transitional Living is the right next move right now, or whether something else we offer (or someone else's program) would be a better fit.

- **Step 2:** **H3:** Sit-down conversation
  **Body:** If it looks like a fit, we'll set up a longer conversation — at our office, over the phone, or wherever it's easiest. We want to understand your story, your goals, what's worked and what hasn't, and what you'd want a week here to feel like. You'll meet some of the people who'd be part of your week, and you'll get a real look at the home.

- **Step 3:** **H3:** Move in and settle
  **Body:** Once there's a bed and we've both said yes, we'll set a move-in date, get you oriented to the house, and introduce you to your case manager and housemates. Your first week is mostly about landing — getting your room set up, learning the rhythm, getting a few essentials in place. The real work starts in week two.

You don't need a specific diagnosis to apply. You don't need to have it all figured out. What you need is to be choosing this — and willing to do the work it takes to make this place work for you.

## Per-service template variations

A few places where Transitional Living diverges from the crisis-stabilization template shape and how I've handled them:

- **Stats row Card 1 swapped to a duration band.** Crisis-stab uses "3–5 Day Stay" — short and exact. Community Support uses "Ongoing | Long-term, not short-term" — open-ended. Transitional Living sits between them and the page must say so up front: **"Weeks to months."** This single card carries more weight than any other on the page; once Justin confirms a number (e.g., "30–180 days," "average 90 days"), swap the placeholder for the real range.
- **Stats row Card 2 reframed to answer the lockdown question.** Crisis-stab's "24/7 Always Available" is about staffing. Ours adds three words — *"never locked, always supported"* — that quietly defuse the single biggest fear about a residential program. Worth keeping even if Card 2 gets re-templated.
- **"What you can expect" reorganized as a day-arc.** Crisis-stab and CSP both list *types of support* (case management, life skills, recovery planning, peer support). For Transitional Living the more useful organizing principle is *what your day actually feels like* — because the persona's biggest unanswered question (after duration) is *"what will I be doing all day?"* I kept four cards but reframed them as morning/planning/skills/community rather than four parallel service categories.
- **"What we ask of you" leads with *choose to be here.*** Crisis-stab's version emphasizes attendance/compliance for a 3–5 day stay. CSP's emphasizes participation and self-direction. Transitional Living's first ask has to be *voluntariness* — because if we don't name it, the reader assumes the opposite.
- **Admissions Step 2 expanded.** Crisis-stab Step 2 is brief (assessment). For Transitional Living, the "sit-down conversation" is doing more work — it's also a tour, a meet-the-team, and a place to ask the questions a long stay deserves. The longer copy is intentional.
- **Eligibility includes a de-funnel.** Pointing readers to crisis stabilization or CSP if they're on the wrong page. This is the right move for any service where misrouting is a real risk; for Transitional Living it's nearly guaranteed.
- **"What to bring" dropped from this draft.** Crisis-stab includes a packing list. Transitional Living will need one too (probably longer — it's a longer stay), but the right list is highly logistics-dependent and I'd rather Justin confirm what's actually allowed and expected in the home before I draft it. Placeholder note: *TODO: pack list once house rules are confirmed.*
- **Closing CTA panel can be reused from crisis-stab with a substitution.** Replace the headline with **"Ready to land somewhere steady?"** — keeps the verb "land" from the hero, doesn't promise a treatment outcome, and frames the call as a low-cost ask.

## Open questions for Justin

1. **Length of stay.** This is the most important open question on the page. What's the real range — 30 days, 30–90, 60–180, up to a year? Even an average ("typical stay is around X months") would unlock the hero stats row. Until we have a number, the page leads with "weeks to months."
2. **Capacity.** How many beds does the Woodward Transitional Living home have? Eyerly Ball's Cummins/Francis House each have 15 — useful comparator. Would help inform whether wait time is a meaningful issue to acknowledge in admissions.
3. **Locations.** The current HTML page implies Woodward only. Is that correct? If Perry or Knoxville will also offer Transitional Living (now or at Knoxville's Spring 2026 opening), the locations card and several copy moments need to reflect that.
4. **Funding / fees.** Is Transitional Living Medicaid-funded? Habilitation-services category (HCBS waiver)? Self-pay? Mixed? Per Justin's 2026-05-22 directive, the current draft says nothing about pricing — but admissions reality means we'll need a position eventually. *Deferred until Justin decides the funding-conversation pattern across all services.*
5. **Eligibility criteria — formal vs. soft.** Right now the page says "stepping out of an intensive setting" loosely. Is there a formal upstream-care requirement (e.g., must come from inpatient, must have completed crisis stabilization, must have a habilitation-services eligibility determination)? If yes, the eligibility section needs to be more specific without sounding gatekeepery.
6. **Substance use rules.** I wrote "substance-free home" into the asks. Is that accurate? If yes, is it sober-house-strict (random screens, immediate discharge for use) or more case-by-case? The wrong framing here loses people from one of two directions — too strict and people in recovery won't apply; too loose and people who need a sober environment won't trust the home.
7. **Visitor / leave policy.** "You can see your family, hold a job, take a walk" — confirm. Are there overnight passes? Curfew? Sign-in/sign-out? The page asserts non-lockdown; we need to know the actual operational version of that.
8. **Staffing model.** "Support staff in the house around the clock" — confirm the title we should use. Residential support specialists? Direct support professionals? Recovery coaches? Case managers? I'm using "support staff" + "your case manager" generically.
9. **Pack list.** What can/should residents bring on move-in day? Crisis-stab has a list; we'll want one too. Likely longer (longer stay = more stuff: seasonal clothing, books, photos, comfort items, work materials).
10. **Step-down destinations.** What does "next place" usually look like for people moving on? Their own apartment? Supported housing? Family? Recovery housing? Knowing the mix would let us write more specifically about the arc of a stay and what week-by-week progress points toward.
11. **House rules / structure level.** Some transitional living programs run more like a structured residential treatment program (group times, chore charts, daily check-ins); others run more like a sober house or a supported apartment with light-touch staffing. Where does ours sit on that spectrum? The "What you can expect" section is currently written closer to the middle of that spectrum.
12. **Co-occurring / specialty fit.** Does this home serve people with substance use co-occurring with mental health? People with significant medical needs? People with prior incarceration? The page implies yes-to-all; confirm before we publish.
13. **Service-specific phone vs. 844-428-3878.** The current HTML uses the Woodward direct line (515-642-4125). Per Justin's 2026-05-22 directive 844-428-3878 is the primary routing number; I've used it throughout. Confirm 844 is the right entry point for *non-crisis* Transitional Living inquiries, or whether we should expose a separate admissions number as a secondary CTA.
14. **Photos.** A residential program lives or dies on its photos — the kitchen, the common room, a bedroom, the porch. None of the existing pages have real photography yet. Naming this in case it triggers a photo-priority decision sooner than later.

## Sources

- [Foundation 2 Crisis Services](https://foundation2.org/) — Iowa peer. Strong crisis-line and mobile-crisis brand; *no transitional living offering.* Cited primarily as context — the dominant Iowa crisis brand hands the persona off exactly where this service catches them.
- [CommUnity Crisis Services](https://builtbycommunity.org/) — Iowa peer. Runs *Healing Prairie Farm,* a farm-based mental health program; public-facing copy on the Farm itself is thin. Useful for confirming Iowans are searching for residential-adjacent options outside hospitals.
- [CommUnity — Ways We Can Help](https://builtbycommunity.org/i-need-help/) — Iowa peer. Hub page listing CommUnity's full service mix; useful for seeing how a multi-service org sequences crisis vs. residential-adjacent offerings.
- [UnityPoint Health – Eyerly Ball](https://www.unitypoint.org/locations/unitypoint-health---eyerly-ball) — Iowa peer. The most directly comparable residential operator: Cummins House and Francis House (15 beds each, 24/7), plus habilitation homes in the community. Source for *what content belongs* on this page; counter-example for *how it should sound.* Eligibility bullets are exactly the funding-gate framing we're not doing.
- [Optimae LifeServices](https://optimaelifeservices.com/) — Iowa peer. *"At your side. On your side."* is the warmest line of copy from any Iowa peer in this category. Their motto is the tonal target.
- [Optimae — Behavioral Health Services](https://optimaelifeservices.com/services/behavioral-health/) — Iowa peer. Confirms the Iowa residential-services category landscape; cautionary tale on losing tonal warmth as the page goes deeper into clinical detail.
- [Iowa HHS — Targeted Case Management](https://hhs.iowa.gov/health-prevention/disability-services/tcm) — Policy source. Vocabulary to translate *away from* (RCF/MI, supported community living, habilitation, integrated plan of care) but also vocabulary our *referrers* (hospital social workers, probation officers, county case managers) recognize.
- [Gould Farm (Massachusetts)](https://www.gouldfarm.org/) — Outside-Iowa benchmark. The country's oldest therapeutic community for adults recovering from mental illness; the gold standard for the *dignity-of-work, community-as-treatment* register. Borrow the posture (work as recovery, real relationships, meaningful days, *"guests"* not patients); do not borrow the rural fee-for-service positioning.
- [Fountain House (NYC)](https://www.fountainhouse.org/) — Outside-Iowa benchmark. Mission line — *"so we can all live with purpose, belonging, and dignity"* — sets the three-noun bar for what a service page in this category should aim for emotionally. The clubhouse model's side-by-side participation principle informs the *"choose to be here"* posture in our asks.
- [Pathways Housing First](https://www.pathwayshousingfirst.org/) — Outside-Iowa benchmark. Cited (also in the CSP draft) for its non-coercive, no-precondition framing. Backstop for ensuring our admissions copy never reads like a list of hurdles. Distinct from our model — we're transitional, they're permanent — but tonally aligned.
- [community-support-program (sibling wiki page)](community-support-program.md) — Internal cross-reference. The Eligibility de-funnel points readers there if they need someone to come to them at home rather than the other way around.
