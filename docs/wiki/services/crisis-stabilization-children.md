## Persona

I'm a parent in Iowa, somewhere between Perry and Des Moines, sitting on the floor outside my 14-year-old's bedroom at 11pm. Tonight she said she didn't want to be here anymore. I don't know if she meant it the way I'm afraid she meant it. I don't know if "crisis" is a strong enough word or too strong a word. I have done five Google searches in the last twenty minutes and every page so far has either tried to sell me a hotline membership, walked me through the warning signs I already know she has, or showed me a building that looks like a hospital with a parking lot full of cop cars. I do not want my kid waiting on a gurney in an ER hallway for nine hours. I do not want a uniformed deputy showing up at the house and making this the thing the neighbors talk about. I want to know — in the first ten seconds of looking at the page — whether the people on the other end of the phone will (a) take a kid this age, (b) actually have a bed tonight or tomorrow, (c) let me stay involved, and (d) not treat me like I'm the reason this happened. I am the lens for every word on this page. But the page also has to work for the parent of a six-year-old whose meltdowns are getting dangerous, the grandma raising her grandson, the school counselor calling about a sophomore she's worried about, and the pediatrician at the end of a long appointment who just wants somewhere safe to send a family tonight.

## Research summary

I reviewed five Iowa peers — **CommUnity Crisis Services / Healing Prairie Farm** (Iowa City, the only Iowa youth crisis stabilization page that genuinely reads like it was written for a scared parent), **Foundation 2 Crisis Services** (Cedar Rapids — runs the only emergency youth shelter in Linn County, ages 11–17; strong "trusted support" framing on the homepage), **Tanager Place** (Cedar Rapids — site was firewalled behind Cloudflare during the research session, treated as "could not assess"), **Orchard Place** (Des Moines — Iowa's oldest children's mental health charity; runs the PMIC residential campus and a wide outpatient menu; voice is warm-corporate, not crisis-immediate), and **Four Oaks** (Cedar Rapids, statewide — operates QRTP and PMIC residential programs; the public website is built almost entirely around acronyms and operational categories, not around a parent in crisis). Two outside-Iowa benchmarks: **NAMI's parent-and-family crisis materials** (the best plain-language framework for what a parent should be doing tonight — though the site itself is structurally too donor-and-membership-forward to land for a parent in the moment), and **Boston Children's Hospital's psychiatric services** (Cloudflare-blocked during fetch; treated as "not assessed" rather than fabricated). Two Iowa HHS policy documents on **Crisis Stabilization Residential Services — Child** and **Supporting Youth in Crisis** confirm the regulatory shape: under-five-day stays, awake 24/7 staff, voluntary, "least-restrictive setting" language — and they read exactly like the bureaucratic register we are not allowed to use. To the persona at 11pm, the single most reassuring page on the Iowa internet is CommUnity's Healing Prairie Farm FAQ (Can my kid bring their phone? Can I visit? What should they pack? Will they go to school? Will they share a room? Will they actually get their meds?) — it is the only one that answers the questions a parent will literally type into Google. We should model the children's page on that FAQ instinct, not on the adult crisis-stab template lift-and-shift.

## Tone notes

- **Speak to the parent. Not the child.** This page is read overwhelmingly by adults calling on behalf of a child, not by the child themselves. Every "you" on the page is the parent/guardian/caller. (The HTML draft today has one moment that addresses the child — *"Your child is not alone in this"* — and that is fine. But the body needs to address the caller.)
- **Disarm the shame in the first 100 words.** A parent calling about a child has, almost universally, been quietly carrying the belief that they should have prevented this. Say plainly: this is not because you failed. Don't dwell — say it once, mean it, move on.
- **Name the age range, fast.** "Ages 5–17" should appear in the H1 or the dek, not buried. The age floor (5) is unusual — it sets us apart from Foundation 2 (11+), Healing Prairie Farm (10+), and most adolescent units (12+). Parents of younger kids will leave a page that doesn't say their age is welcome.
- **Don't fork the copy by age.** The page must serve the parent of a 6-year-old and the parent of a 16-year-old without splitting into "Children (5–12)" and "Teens (13–17)" sections. The handle: write the program in terms of *what we do* (age-appropriate one-on-one time, family involvement, room set-up, schoolwork) rather than developmental stages. One brief acknowledgment up top — "Kids at five and teens at seventeen are not the same — and the program is built to meet each kid at their age, not somewhere in between" — and then drop the bifurcation.
- **Answer the FAQ a parent will actually Google.** Lifted from Healing Prairie Farm's FAQ and the questions I've heard parents ask: *Can I visit? Can they keep their phone? Will they get their meds? Will they share a room? Will they keep up with school? What do I pack?* Answer each one — short and named.
- **Name schools and pediatricians once, by name, as referral pathways.** Without making them the *primary* route. A teacher reading this should feel addressed; a parent reading this should not feel like they need a teacher's permission to call.
- **Never name a diagnosis.** Per Justin's 2026-05-22 directive. Refer to "what they're going through," "the hard thing they're carrying," "a mental health crisis." Not depression, anxiety, ODD, suicidality-as-a-noun. The Iowa HHS PDFs and NAMI both lead with diagnostic vocabulary; we lead with experience.
- **Never name pricing, insurance, or Medicaid.** Per Justin's 2026-05-22 directive. Funding is an open question and stays one until Justin says otherwise. The current HTML draft says *"You don't need insurance, you don't need a referral"* — strip that line on rewrite.
- **Avoid healthcare-blue voice.** No "continuum of care," no "treatment modalities," no "consumer," no "acute presentation," no "behavioral health" as a clinical adjective. Read the Eyerly Ball treatment in the [Community Support Program draft](community-support-program.md) for the full counter-example list.
- **Cops and uniforms.** This page does not need to make a statement about law enforcement — but the unspoken question for many parents is *do I have to call the police?* Answer it implicitly by being the alternative. The hero CTA is the answer.

### What is this program?
- **H2:** A short stay, a soft landing.
- **Body:**
  > Crisis Stabilization for Children is a short-term, voluntary stay — usually three to five days — for kids and teens between the ages of 5 and 17 who are in a mental health crisis. It is not a hospital. It is not a juvenile facility. It is a small, home-like setting with awake staff around the clock and people whose whole job is to know how to be with young people on their hardest days.
  >
  > Kids at five and teens at seventeen are not the same — and the program is built to meet each child at their age, not somewhere in between. Younger kids get more play, more art, more help finding the words. Older kids get more space to talk, to be heard without being lectured, to feel like an adult is actually listening. Everybody gets a calm room, predictable meals, time outdoors, and a person who notices them.
  >
  > Stays are voluntary. Kids are not brought here against their will, and they can't be held against yours. What we ask is a few days to help your child feel steadier — and to help you, the people who love them, figure out together what comes next.

### What you can expect (for your child)
- **H2:** What your child's days look like here
- **Intro:** Every kid is met where they are. But the rhythm of a day here is meant to feel predictable, low-pressure, and human.
- **Cards (4):**
  1. **Icon hint:** Person + speech bubble *(one-on-one)* | **H3:** Time with someone trained for this | **Body:** Daily one-on-one time with a clinician or crisis specialist who works with young people every day. Kids talk if they want to. They draw, walk, or just sit with someone if they don't. Both count.
  2. **Icon hint:** Pill bottle with check *(meds)* | **H3:** Their medications, on schedule | **Body:** Any prescribed medications continue on their normal schedule. Bring them in their original containers — our staff will keep them safely and make sure each dose happens on time.
  3. **Icon hint:** Small group circle *(groups)* | **H3:** Small, age-appropriate groups | **Body:** Daily small-group time built around things kids and teens can actually use: coping skills, grounding, creative outlets, talking with peers who get it. Group is never the main event for a kid who isn't ready.
  4. **Icon hint:** Sun over open ground *(rest)* | **H3:** Real rest, real food, real space | **Body:** A quiet bedroom (usually shared with one other kid the same age), three meals a day, outdoor time, and a daily rhythm that doesn't depend on screens or stimulation. Sometimes the most therapeutic thing is a week of sleep.

### What we ask of you (the parent or guardian)
- **H2:** What we ask of you
- **Intro:** You are not stepping out of this. You are stepping into a few days of help. Here's what helps us help your child.
- **Cards (3):**

  3. **Icon hint:** Calendar | **H3:** Be honest about what you've been carrying | **Body:** What's been going on at home, at school, with sleep, with friends — even the parts you wish weren't true. Our team isn't here to judge you. The more we know, the better we can help your child land softly.

### What to bring
- **H2:** What to pack for your child
- **Intro:** A short list. We have what you don't bring.
- **Checklist (parents can screenshot):**
  - Comfortable clothes and pajamas for 3–5 days (washer and dryer on-site if you want to pack less).
  - All current medications in their original prescription bottles.
  - A comfort item — a stuffed animal, a blanket, a favorite hoodie. Anything that already smells like home.

## Per-service template variations

Where Children's Crisis Stabilization diverges from the adult crisis stabilization template, and how I've handled each variation:

- **Voice flip: written to the parent, not the patient.** Every "you" on the adult page is the person staying. Every "you" on this page is the adult calling about a child. The one exception — the H1 sub-line speaks "to" the child briefly — is held because it sets emotional posture, not because the page is for the child.
- **"What you can expect" is *for your child*; "What we ask of you" is *of the parent.*** Two parallel sections, each labeled so the reader knows whose role is whose. The adult page collapses both into one perspective.
- **FAQ section added.** The adult page doesn't have one. Children's pages live or die on the parent-Googled questions: visits, phones, medications, rooms, school. We answer them on-page rather than burying them in the call.
- **"What to bring" expanded into both a checklist and a card.** Adults bring less; kids' parents pack with more anxiety. Naming "a comfort item — a stuffed animal, a blanket, a favorite hoodie" does enormous emotional work for a parent who is otherwise frozen at the dresser.
- **Age-range positioning made explicit in the H1 sub.** Adult page is "adults" without further qualification. Children's page must declare "ages 5 through 17" near the top because the floor (5) is unusual in Iowa and the ceiling (17) is the question for parents of teens about to age out.
- **"Voluntary" stated plainly.** The adult page can rely on adult autonomy as an assumption. For a child, the question *Will you take her against my will? Will you keep her against ours?* is unspoken and load-bearing. The answer (voluntary, both directions) appears in the body and the FAQ.
- **Schools and pediatricians explicitly named as callers.** Adult page doesn't need this. For children, naming them once disarms the "do I need permission?" worry and acknowledges the reality of how referrals reach us.
- **"What this costs" deliberately deferred.** Per Justin's 2026-05-22 directive: no pricing, no insurance, no Medicaid copy. The adult template's old "no insurance, no referral required" line is dropped from this draft entirely. Replace with a confirmed funding line when Justin gives one.
- **Provider callout (in the existing HTML) kept.** The bar pointing referring partners to the Provider Hub stays; pediatricians, schools, and ER discharge planners are a meaningful share of callers for the children's program.
- **24/7 stat kept (unlike on the Community Support Program page where it was dropped).** For crisis stabilization, around-the-clock awake staff is not a marketing claim — it is the central operational fact a parent is screening for.

## Generalization check

Re-reading section by section against personas other than the 11pm parent of a 14-year-old:

- **Hero.** "Crisis Stabilization program is a short-term, voluntary stay for kids and teens ages 5 through 17 who are going through a mental health crisis" — works whether the kid is 5, 11, or 17, whether the caller is the parent, the grandparent, the foster parent, the school counselor, the pediatrician. The reassurance line spells out the caller types explicitly. ✅
- **Stats row.** Length of stay, age band, awake 24/7. All structural facts. No scenario assumed. ✅
- **What is this program?** Re-read as a grandparent raising a six-year-old grandson: the "younger kids get more play, more art, more help finding the words" line lands. Re-read as the parent of a 17-year-old about to age out: "Older kids get more space to talk, to be heard without being lectured" lands. The bifurcation is handled in one paragraph rather than forking the page. ✅
- **What you can expect.** Card 2 (medications) explicitly handles "if your child is on any prescribed medications" rather than assuming they are — the parent of a kid who isn't on meds reads past it cleanly. Card 1 (talk-or-draw-or-sit) handles a 5-year-old, an autistic 10-year-old, and a 17-year-old who refuses to engage. ✅
- **What we ask of you.** Card 3 ("Be honest about what you've been carrying") is the most persona-loaded card on the page. Re-read as a foster parent: still lands, because "what's been going on at home" includes "what we know about their previous home." Re-read as a school counselor calling about a student: this card is one they will skip because it's clearly aimed at family — and that's fine, because the section is labeled *for the parent or guardian*. ✅
- **What to bring.** Photo ID for "parent or guardian" — works for grandparent, foster parent, legal guardian. Reads cleanly for non-traditional family structures. The comfort item line is gender-, age-, and culture-neutral. ✅
- **FAQ.** Re-read as a parent of a 6-year-old: "Will they keep up with school?" — kindergartener-relevant. "Can they have their phone?" — likely not the parent's first question for a 6-year-old, but a non-issue (no phone, no problem). Re-read as the parent of a 17-year-old: phone access is the *first* question. Same FAQ answers both. ✅
- **How to get started.** Step 1 names the caller broadly ("trained person picks up at any hour"). Steps 2 and 3 use "you bring your child in," which assumes a parent/guardian. For a school counselor calling about a student, the flow would have the school looping in the family before arrival — that's accurate and standard, but a future iteration could add a one-line "Calling about a student? We coordinate the family handoff with you." ⚠️ Minor — flagging for Justin (Open Question §10).
- **Who can call.** Existed primarily for this generalization purpose. Lists every realistic caller type explicitly. ✅
- **Eligibility.** "Kids and teens ages 5 through 17 in a mental health crisis who need a few days of focused, voluntary support outside of home." No diagnosis. No income. No prerequisite. ✅
- **Closing CTA.** "You know your child" — assumes the caller is the child's primary attachment figure. A school counselor calling about a student is technically excluded by this line; in practice, the closing is the last beat of the parent narrative and the school counselor will have already decided to call by the time they reach it. ✅

Net: one minor flag in How to Get Started, otherwise clean.

## Open questions for Justin

1. **Facility, location, and capacity.** Where does Children's Crisis Stabilization happen — Woodward, Perry, or a separate building? How many beds for ages 5–17? Is the children's program physically separated from the adult program (same campus, different wing? entirely separate site?), and is that separation something we should state on the page?
2. **Age floor confirmation.** Is **5** the real lower bound, or is the practical floor higher (most peer programs are 10+ or 12+)? The age range is a load-bearing differentiator; we need to be honest about it.
3. **Funding / payment language.** Per the 2026-05-22 directive, no pricing or insurance copy is on this draft. When ready: is the program Medicaid-funded only? Sliding-scale for uninsured families? Grant-covered? The current HTML draft contains *"You don't need insurance"* — that line should come out of the rewrite unless we can stand behind it.
4. **Service-specific phone or single 844 routing.** Is 844-428-3878 the right number for parents calling about a child, or is there a separate intake line for the children's program? If single number — confirm the routing on the operator side ("press 2 for children's stabilization" or similar) so the page can promise it confidently.
5. **Voluntary status confirmation.** Iowa HHS framework states crisis stabilization is voluntary. Confirm Inside Out's children's program operates the same way (no involuntary holds at intake). If there are circumstances where it doesn't, we need a one-sentence honest carve-out rather than a vague claim.
6. **Family visit policy specifics.** The FAQ promises scheduled visits. What's the real cadence — daily, every other day, by appointment only? Any minimum age for younger siblings visiting?
7. **Phone / device policy specifics.** The FAQ promises scheduled phone access. Confirm the actual policy — duration per day, areas where allowed, whether parents can call in directly during off-times.
8. **Length of stay.** 3–5 days is a defensible Iowa-standard placeholder. Confirm Inside Out's typical and maximum (some programs go up to 7 or 10 days when a step-down isn't immediately available).
9. **School / education during stay.** The draft says "crisis stabilization is a pause from school by design." Confirm. Some peer programs offer on-site tutoring for longer stays; if Inside Out does, the FAQ answer should change.
10. **School-counselor / pediatrician referral flow.** Is there a dedicated provider intake path (form, fax, secure portal), or do they call the same 844 number? Should the page surface a one-line "Calling about a student or patient? Here's how" callout above the parent-flow steps? (See Generalization Check §How to get started.)
11. **What to bring — items to *not* bring.** Most peer programs maintain a "do not bring" list (anything with cords, sharps, gum, certain medications, etc.). Inside Out's exact list would make the page more useful — or, alternatively, a single sentence "We'll send the full packing list when you call" is honest and lets staff handle nuance by phone.
12. **What we promise siblings / partners / divorced co-parents.** Custody is messy. Do we have a stance on which adults can authorize a voluntary stay and which can't? Doesn't have to be on the public page, but the call-handler needs the answer.
13. **What happens after the stay.** The page hints at "what comes next" but doesn't describe it. Are there formal step-down pathways into outpatient, into community support, into peer support, into transitional living? A one-paragraph "after stabilization" section could ease parent anxiety considerably — flagging as an additive future block, not a P0 for this draft.
14. **Tanager Place benchmark.** Their site was firewalled during research. Worth a second pass before publish — Tanager runs Iowa's most directly comparable PMIC and is the likely first competitor a Cedar-Rapids-area parent will compare us to.
