## Persona

I'm 34. I haven't slept more than three hours a night in over a week. My head has gone somewhere I can't follow, and the people who love me are starting to look scared when they look at me. I am not in immediate danger of hurting myself or anyone else — but I am one bad night away from it, and I know it. I don't want the ER. The last time someone I knew went to the ER for a mental health crisis, they sat 14 hours under fluorescent lights and ended up on a hold they couldn't leave. I don't want lights and sirens. I don't want to be admitted to a hospital. I want a small, quiet, *home-like* place where a real person can sit with me for two or three days while I get my feet back under me, where I am not alone but I am also not being processed. I want to walk in on my own feet, with my own bag in my hand. I want to be able to walk back out. I am the lens for every word on this page — but the page has to work for every adult Iowan who arrives at the same edge I'm at, whether the precipitating thing is grief, exhaustion, a relapse, postpartum, the anniversary of a loss, or nothing namable at all; and it has to work for the spouse, sibling, or parent who is making this call on someone else's behalf.

## Research summary

Reviewed five Iowa-anchored sources — **Foundation 2** (Cedar Rapids), **CommUnity Crisis Services** (Iowa City), **Eyerly Ball / UnityPoint** (Des Moines), **NAMI Iowa**, and the **Iowa HHS / Your Life Iowa** statewide crisis framework (including the Iowa Administrative Code 441—24.39 rule defining "crisis stabilization residential services" / CSRS) — plus two outside-Iowa benchmarks: **Connections Health Solutions** (Arizona / national; the most-cited crisis-stabilization operator in the country, originator of the "no wrong door, anyone-anytime-anything" model) and the **Crisis Now** policy framework (the standards-setter for the three-tier crisis continuum: line → mobile → stabilization).

The single most important finding from the persona's vantage point: every Iowa peer offers a *combination* of crisis services (hotline + mobile + sometimes stabilization), but only a handful operate adult residential stabilization beds, and almost no one writes about that bed in plain English. The Iowa HHS / Your Life Iowa state-level pages describe the *category* of service in flat policy language ("Sometimes adults and youth in crisis need a safe and secure place to go for a few days"). Eyerly Ball treats stabilization as an item buried inside a list of accreditations. Foundation 2's public-facing page focuses on hotline and mobile, with stabilization information available via Your Life Iowa's directory listing rather than as a standalone marketing page. **There is a large empty room in the Iowa market for a stabilization page that talks to a human, not a payer, not a policymaker.** Inside Out's page should walk straight into that room.

The benchmark Connections Health Solutions homepage models the emotional register we should aim for — *"No Judgments. No Exceptions. 24/7 mental health care"* and *"the right mental health crisis care, right when you need it"* — without inventing claims we can't back up.

The Iowa Admin Code (441—24.39) is the source of every cold word we must NOT use — *"voluntary individuals," "stabilize and reintegrate the individual back into the community," "awake and attentive staffing," "short-term stabilization plan,"* — and is therefore valuable mostly as a *translation key* showing what plain-English version of each clause needs to land on the page.

## Tone notes

- **Lead with the choice, not the credentials.** The single biggest emotional differentiator from an ER is that this is *voluntary* — the person walks in and the person can leave. That word "voluntary" reads bureaucratic, so we say it as: *You walk in on your own feet. You can walk back out.* That sentence should appear early and unmistakeably.
- **Draw the contrast with the ER explicitly, but kindly.** The persona is choosing this instead of an ER. Say so. The page can't pretend the ER doesn't exist; it has to give the reader permission to choose differently. (Avoid putting down ER staff — they are sometimes the right call, and they are our partners.)
- **"Home-like" is the magic word.** Lift it from Iowa state policy language. The Inside Out facilities are not hospitals; they are small residential settings. Say so plainly. Show it. (When real photography arrives, this section becomes the most important place to use it.)
- **Name the team members; don't list disciplines.** Instead of *"licensed clinicians, RNs, peer specialists, and care coordinators"* (resume-stack), say *"the people who'll be with you here include therapists who've sat with crises like yours for years, nurses for medication questions, and peer specialists — folks who've been through their own hard things and come out the other side."* Same facts, different register.
- **Acknowledge readiness without shaming it.** Some callers will be at the door reluctantly. Some will be there with family pushing. The page should accept both without judgment — *"You don't have to be sure you belong here. Picking up the phone is the part you have to do; we'll help you figure out the rest."*
- **Never name a diagnosis.** Per Justin's 2026-05-22 directive, copy says *"what you're going through," "what you're living with," "a mental health crisis,"* — never *"depression," "bipolar," "PTSD,"* etc. The stabilization service is for everyone in acute crisis regardless of what's driving it.
- **No pricing claims, no payer mentions, no "insurance shouldn't stop you."** Per Justin's 2026-05-22 directive, funding is an open question. The page does not say or imply anything about insurance, sliding scale, Medicaid, or sliding-fee. The current HTML's line *"You don't need insurance to walk through our doors"* is removed from this draft. Justin will decide funding posture before publish.
- **Family is a real reader, but a secondary one.** Like the CSP page, this page is written primarily *to the person who would stay here*, with one explicit acknowledgement that family members and other providers also make this call.

### What is Crisis Stabilization?
- **H2:** A short stay that can change the trajectory.
- **Body:**
  > Some kinds of mental health crisis aren't an emergency-room emergency, but they aren't something you can ride out alone, either. There is a middle ground — and this is it. Adult Crisis Stabilization is a short, residential program for adults in Iowa who need a few days in a safe and steady place while the worst of what you're going through eases up.
  >
  > A stay is usually three to five days. The setting is small and home-like — not a hospital ward, not a locked unit, not fluorescent lights and a sliding glass partition. You'll have a bed in a quiet room. There's a kitchen, a common space, and people awake in the house at every hour. The people who'll be with you here include therapists who've sat with crises like yours for years, nurses for medication questions, and peer specialists — folks who've been through their own hard things and come out the other side.
  >
  > You come because you choose to. You can leave when you're ready. While you're here, we'll work with you — not on you — on what is actually happening, what would help right now, and what a steadier next week could look like. You don't have to know what's wrong before you arrive. Figuring that out together is most of what we do.

### What you can expect (during your stay)
- **H2:** What the days here look like.
- **Intro:** Every stay shapes itself around the person. But here's the rhythm of what to expect.

  2. **Icon hint:** Open hand with a small pill bottle / gentle medical *(meds without alarm)* | **H3:** Medical & medication support | **Body:** If you take medications, we help keep them on track and a nurse is available for questions or adjustments coordinated with your prescriber. If you don't, we don't push them on you.
  3. **Icon hint:** Small lit lantern / lived experience *(peer)* | **H3:** A peer who's been here | **Body:** A peer specialist on the team has lived through their own mental health crisis and recovery. Sometimes the most useful conversation in the building is the one with the person who has been exactly where you are.
  4. **Icon hint:** Hand with a small notebook *(plan)* | **H3:** A plan you help write | **Body:** Within your first day here, we'll sit down together and write down what would actually help — what to focus on, who to loop in, what comes next when you leave. We'll revisit it as your stay unfolds, because plans should breathe with the people they're for.

### What we ask of you
- **H2:** What we ask of you.
- **Intro:** Not much. But these few things let the stay do its work.
- **Cards (3):**

## Per-service template variations

How this page diverges from the Community Support Program template shape, and why:

- **Stats Card 1 ("3–5 days") stays.** Unlike CSP (which is ongoing, not time-bounded), the *short length of stay* is one of the most important facts about crisis stabilization. It signals "this is a manageable amount of time away from your life" — a critical reassurance for the persona who is hesitating to leave work, kids, or pets behind.
- **Stats Card 3 ("Voluntary") added; replaces "All Iowans Welcome."** The current HTML's "All Iowans Welcome" reads as a welcoming tagline; "Voluntary" carries actual decisional weight for the persona. *Voluntariness* is also the legal-policy heart of CSRS in Iowa (IAC 441—24.39).
- **"What is this?" section explicitly contrasts with hospital / ER.** CSP's "what is this?" section explains where CSP fits among long-term services. This page's equivalent section explains where stabilization fits *between* the ER (too intensive) and going home alone (not safe enough). That contrast is the page's primary job.
- **"What you can expect" cards reshaped around stay rhythm.** CSP's cards described categories of support delivered out in life (in-home visits, paperwork, etc.). This page's cards describe what a *day* inside the program looks like (therapist time, medical support, peer time, planning). The shape of help matches the shape of the service.
- **Peer specialist named explicitly.** Per Connections Health Solutions' best-in-class framing of *"providers and peers with lived experience,"* and per Inside Out's own service catalog including a standalone Peer Support Services line, naming peers as part of the stabilization team is both honest and emotionally load-bearing for the persona. *(If peer specialists are not actually staffed inside the stabilization program — open question for Justin — we cut Card 3.)*
- **"What we ask of you" anchored to staying on-site.** This is the inverse of CSP's framing (CSP is about us coming to you; stabilization is about you coming to us, briefly). The asks reflect that.
- **Admissions Step 3 is "Arrive and settle in"** (matches the existing HTML), where CSP's Step 3 was "Build the plan together."
- **"What to bring" included.** CSP doesn't need a packing list; stabilization does. Keep this checklist — but written warmly, with the parenthetical reassurance that the staff has basics if the person doesn't.
- **Eligibility section stripped of any funding language.** Per directive.

## Generalization check

Re-reading each section in three other personas: (a) a 67-year-old widower whose adult daughter is calling because his grief turned into something she doesn't recognize, (b) a 22-year-old veteran calling for himself after a bad two weeks home, and (c) a hospital social worker calling to refer a patient who is medically clear but not ready to be discharged home alone.

- **Hero.** *"A quiet place to come back to yourself."* / *"A short, voluntary stay … in a small, home-like setting where a steady team can be with you while the worst of it passes."* Reads true for all three. No diagnosis. No family-role assumed. No age signal. The phrase *"come back to yourself"* might land slightly differently for a referring social worker (who is, after all, not "coming back" themselves), but it doesn't exclude — the social worker reads it on behalf of the patient and recognizes it as the right register. ✅

## Partner-provider perspective

Referring partners (ER discharge planners, primary care, mobile crisis teams, law enforcement diverting from booking, psychiatric inpatient stepping people down, judges of mental health court, school counselors of college-age students, VA case managers) read this page differently from a self-caller or family caller. What earns their trust:

1. **Same number as the public.** 844-428-3878 — providers respect simplicity and dislike maintaining separate referral lines for the same service.
2. **Honest length-of-stay window.** *"Usually three to five days"* matches the Iowa state framework (CSRS expected stay <5 days), so an ER discharge planner can plan around it. If Inside Out occasionally accepts shorter or longer stays, the page should hint at that flexibility — but the stated window must be the operational reality, not aspirational.
3. **Clear delineation of what stabilization is NOT.** Providers need to know that stabilization is not an involuntary hold, not an inpatient psychiatric hospital, not a medical detox bed, and not a long-term residential placement. The page can do this gently in the body copy ("Not a hospital. Not an ER.") and more directly in a sidebar for providers.
4. **Voluntariness.** Critical for providers diverting from involuntary commitment paths. The Stats row Card 3 carries this; the body copy reinforces it.
5. **Team composition.** Providers want to know the staff mix (LMSW / LMHC / RN / peer specialist / CISM-trained crisis intervention specialist) — even though the public page avoids resume-stacking. A small "for providers" expansion behind the partner-provider hub can carry the credentials.
6. **Bed availability + intake speed.** ER discharge planners specifically need to know how fast a bed can open. The page should *not* lie or imply same-day availability if that's not always true; an honest "we'll tell you on the phone" beats an aspirational promise.
7. **Step-down pathway.** Crisis stabilization is most useful when followed by a warm handoff to ongoing care (CSP, outpatient, peer support, primary-care provider). Providers want to see that we coordinate the next step, not just discharge people back into the same conditions that brought them in. The "What you can expect — Card 4 (A plan you help write)" implicitly covers this; the partner-provider sidebar can make it explicit.

> Referring a patient or community member to Adult Crisis Stabilization? Same line as the public — 844-428-3878 — or use the Provider Hub for non-urgent referrals and a downloadable intake checklist.
