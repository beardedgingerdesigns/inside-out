## Persona

I'm somewhere in the middle of my own recovery. Not the worst week of my life — but not steady, either. I've already tried the things people tell you to try. I sat through a few months of therapy and could never quite say the part out loud that actually mattered. I downloaded the apps. I went to a support group once where eight strangers sat in a circle and a facilitator with a clipboard asked us to "share if we felt comfortable." I didn't. I'm not in a crisis tonight. I'm not looking for a clinician. I'm looking for one human being who has actually walked through what I'm walking through and is still standing — someone I can talk to without translating everything I say into clinical vocabulary first. I'm the lens for every word on this page, but the page itself has to serve every Iowan who lands here: someone newly out of a stabilization stay who needs a steady hand; someone whose family is gently pushing them to "do something"; someone five years into recovery who just wants company on the long road; and someone who tried clinical care and bounced off it. None of them needs to be diagnosed at again. All of them need to feel less alone before the page is over.

## Research summary

I reviewed seven Iowa and national peer support providers — **CommUnity Crisis Services** (Iowa City — peer phrasing already established in the Inside Out CSP draft), **Foundation 2** (Cedar Rapids — 988 partner, runs Iowa's warm line model), **NAMI Iowa** and **NAMI National** (peer-led Connection groups + the Peer-to-Peer education course), **Iowa HHS / Iowa Peer Workforce Collaborative** (the policy + credentialing layer behind Certified Peer Specialists in Iowa), **Optimae LifeServices** (a large Iowa behavioral-health provider that names peer support inside an enormous service catalog), and **Doors to Wellbeing** (the Copeland Center's SAMHSA-funded national peer workforce technical-assistance center). I added two outside-Iowa benchmarks — **Mental Health America (MHA)** for the cleanest national-level definitional language, and **Fountain House** (NYC, the originating clubhouse model) for the deepest cultural example of a peer-driven recovery community. The single most useful pattern: every credible peer-support page in the field defines the role *by who delivers it*, not by what it delivers. The weakest pages (Optimae, Eyerly Ball-style hospital sites) bury peer support inside a catalog of "supportive services" without ever saying what makes a peer different. The strongest (NAMI Connection, Doors to Wellbeing, Fountain House) lead with lived experience as the actual deliverable — "led by people who've been there," "peer-led initiatives," "members helping members." This page has to own the explainer "what is a peer specialist?" because no Iowa peer provider I found does it well, and the rest of the field assumes the reader already knows.

That is the whole offer, in nine words.   - ❌ Didn't: NAMI Iowa's broader site routes hard to "Join NAMI" / "Donate" before it routes anyone to help. Useful counter-pattern: don't make the warmest service on our site sit behind a membership ask.

- **Iowa HHS / Iowa Peer Workforce Collaborative** (https://hhs.iowa.gov/iowa-peer-workforce-collaborative — and related Iowa policy pages)
  - ✅ Landed: Iowa formally credentials Certified Peer Specialists (CPS) through a defined training pathway (multi-day course, supervised hours, written exam, ethics + recovery-story requirement). That credential is the legitimate basis for naming our peers as *certified* peer support specialists on the page — it's not marketing language, it's a real Iowa workforce category.
  - ✅ Landed: The CPS scope of practice is intentionally narrow and *not* clinical — peers don't diagnose, don't prescribe, don't deliver therapy. They share recovery, model coping, navigate systems, and build hope. That distinction must be on the page in plain language so the reader knows what they're getting and what they're not.
  - ❌ Didn't: The HHS / IPWC public-facing pages are written for workforce-development administrators, not for the public being served. They're full of "workforce," "credentialing pipeline," "stakeholders," "billing-eligible service." Translate every phrase. Cite the credential, don't quote the policy.

- **Optimae LifeServices** (https://optimaelifeservices.com/)
  - ❌ Didn't: Peer support is mentioned across a sprawling service catalog (mental health, IDD, home health, residential, employment, peer support) without a dedicated page that explains what their peers do. Reads like the service exists for billing purposes more than for a person looking for it.
  - ✅ Landed (as counter-example): The fact that Iowa's largest behavioral-health provider doesn't have a clean public-facing peer-support page means Inside Out has room to own the explainer. There's no incumbent to outrank — there's a gap.

- **Doors to Wellbeing** (https://www.doorstowellbeing.org/) — outside-Iowa benchmark / national TA center
  - ✅ Landed: *"Doors to Wellbeing... is dedicated to peer-led initiatives and training supporting the peer support workforce."* — Reframes peer support as a *workforce*, not a hobby or a volunteer-of-the-week activity. That subtly elevates how we should describe Inside Out's peer team.
  - ✅ Landed: *"Individual Empowerment... Peer-led Evidence Based Training... Youth Leadership Development."* — Affirms that the peer model is taken seriously at the national level, evidence-based, and meant for a broad age range (we should flag youth peer support as an open question — Iowa has a separate youth-CPS credential path).
  - ❌ Didn't: Federal-grant voice ("SAMHSA-funded National Technical Assistance Center") — doesn't translate to a consumer page. Internal reference only.

- **Mental Health America (MHA) — Peer Support** (https://mhanational.org/peer-support) — outside-Iowa benchmark
  - ✅ Landed: *"Peer Support refers to receiving help from individuals who have suffered from similar experiences."* — One sentence. The plainest definition in the field. The Inside Out page should open with something just as short, but warmer.
  - ❌ Didn't: The MHA page itself is just a short stub treated as a "treatment option" — useful definitionally, weak on what peer support *feels* like or who it's *for*. We're filling that gap, not borrowing from it.

- **Fountain House (NYC)** (https://www.fountainhouse.org/) — outside-Iowa benchmark / clubhouse model
  - ✅ Landed: *"Transformative Mental Illness Recovery Through Community and Advocacy... so we can all live with purpose, belonging, and dignity."* — Three nouns at the end that no Iowa peer provider has put together: *purpose, belonging, dignity.* That trio is what peer support uniquely delivers and clinical care often can't. Worth echoing in spirit.
  - ✅ Landed: The clubhouse model is fundamentally peer-driven — members do the work of running the place — which proves that "peer" is not a soft adjacent role to clinical care; in a healthy peer model, peers are the work.
  - ❌ Didn't: Fountain House calls the people it serves *"members,"* which works in their context but in Iowa would read as foreign jargon. We should not adopt it.

## Tone notes

- **Define "peer specialist" early and plainly.** This page must answer "what is a peer specialist?" in the first scrollable section — not at the bottom in an FAQ. The persona has heard the phrase and isn't sure what it means; the family member calling has never heard it. Both need to leave the first screen understanding *who they will be talking to*.
- **Lived experience is the entire differentiator. Lean into it without making it gimmicky.** Don't dramatize anyone's recovery story for marketing effect. Don't say "our peers have been to hell and back." Say: trained Iowans who have walked their own recovery, are still walking it, and know what it is to be where you are.
- **Answer "is this instead of therapy?" honestly.** No. And also: for some people, yes — for now, or instead, or alongside. The page should not push toward therapy or pull away from it. Position peer support as something that *works with whatever else you're doing or not doing.*
- **Confidentiality is heart-language here.** The persona's biggest unspoken fear is that what they say will end up in a record somewhere. We can't make absolute legal claims about confidentiality on a marketing page, but we can say *plainly* that peer conversations are confidential and that peer specialists don't write things down in a clinical chart the way therapists do. (Confirm scope with Justin.)
- **No diagnosis named.** Use *"what you're going through," "what you're living with," "what you're working through."* Never name a condition by name on this page.
- **No "us vs. clinicians" framing.** Iowa's clinical workforce refers people to peer support and vice versa. Don't position peer support against therapy. Position it as a parallel door.
- **No savior tone, no rescue arc.** Peers are not heroes and the person reading is not a problem to be solved. The relationship is two adults walking together.
- **Family/loved-one caller acknowledged once, briefly.** Like the CSP page: most peer-support callers are calling for themselves, but a meaningful slice is a parent, sibling, partner, or friend. Name them once in "How to get started." Don't make the page about them.
- **Avoid healthcare-blue clinical voice.** Re-read everything against the Eyerly Ball treatment in the CSP draft. If a sentence could appear on a hospital marketing site, rewrite it.

### What is peer support? (the explainer this page must own)
- **H2:** What a peer specialist actually is
- **Body:**
  > A peer support specialist is a trained Iowan who has lived through their own mental health recovery — and who's been credentialed by the state to walk alongside others doing the same thing. In Iowa, that credential is called a Certified Peer Specialist. It means our peers have completed a state-recognized training, work under professional supervision, and follow a code of ethics. It also means something simpler and more important: when they sit down with you, they're not reading from a textbook. They've been on your side of the table.
  >
  > Peer support is not therapy. Our peers don't diagnose, don't prescribe medication, and don't write notes that end up in a clinical chart. They aren't a replacement for a therapist or a doctor — and if you already have one, your peer specialist works around them, not over them. What a peer *does* is the thing that's hardest to find anywhere else: they listen without flinching, they share what worked and what didn't from their own road, and they help you put one foot in front of the other when the day asks you to.
  >
  > For some people, peer support is the thing that finally breaks through after years of trying everything else. For others, it's what they reach for first, before they're ready for a clinical setting. There's no wrong way to start.

### What you can expect
- **H2:** What this can look like, week to week
- **Intro:** Every peer relationship is different — built around what you're working on and what you actually want. Here's the shape of what most people end up doing.
- **Cards (4):**
  1. **Icon hint:** Two chairs facing each other / coffee cups *(one-on-one)* | **H3:** One-on-one conversations | **Body:** Regular time set aside for just the two of you — at our office, at a coffee shop, over the phone, on a walk. Whatever lets you actually talk. No agenda you have to perform around.
  2. **Icon hint:** Speech bubble with a heart *(honest talk)* | **H3:** Talking about the real thing | **Body:** Your peer can hold the things that feel too heavy to put on a therapist's desk, the things you've never told your family, and the stuff you don't have the words for yet. They've held their own version of it. You don't have to translate.
  3. **Icon hint:** Map / compass *(navigation)* | **H3:** Figuring out what comes next | **Body:** Need a therapist and don't know where to start? Trying to get back to work? Sorting through what recovery even looks like for you? Your peer helps you map it — using what they learned the hard way, and what Iowa actually has to offer.
  4. **Icon hint:** Hand with a small flame / candle *(hope)* | **H3:** Hope that's earned, not performed | **Body:** When someone who has been where you are tells you it gets steadier, you can hear it. That's the part peer support gives you that almost nothing else can.

### What we ask of you
- **H2:** What we ask of you
- **Intro:** Peer support works when both people show up honestly. This is what we'll ask.
- **Cards (3):**
  1. **Icon hint:** Open door | **H3:** Say what's actually going on | **Body:** Your peer can't help with the version of your life you'd present at a job interview. We ask that you bring the version that's real — knowing your peer has brought their own real version too. Nothing is too small or too late to talk about.
  2. **Icon hint:** Calendar with a checkmark | **H3:** Keep showing up | **Body:** Some weeks you'll feel like coming. Some weeks you won't. The relationship builds in the showing up, especially on the days that feel hardest. We'll work with what's real — not what's ideal — but the rhythm matters.
  3. **Icon hint:** Two-way arrows | **H3:** Let this be a relationship, not a service | **Body:** Your peer specialist isn't a vending machine for advice. The work is mutual. You tell us what's helping. You tell us when it isn't. You stay the driver of what your own recovery looks like.

### How to get started
- **H2:** How to get started
- **Step 1:** **H3:** Call us | **Body:** Call **844-428-3878**. You can be calling for yourself, or for someone you love — family members, partners, friends, and providers all make this call. We'll listen first, ask a few questions, and tell you honestly whether peer support is the right next step for you. If something else would help more, we'll point you there.
- **Step 2:** **H3:** Meet your peer | **Body:** We'll match you with a peer specialist whose own road has something in common with yours — and we'll set up a first conversation. You can meet at our office, somewhere in the community, or on the phone — whichever feels easier to start. If the match doesn't feel right, you can ask for a different one. That's allowed.
- **Step 3:** **H3:** Find your rhythm | **Body:** Together, you'll figure out how often to meet and what to focus on first. There's no fixed program length — peer support is for as long as it's helping, and we stop when you're ready. The pace belongs to you.

### Eligibility
- **H2:** Who this is for
- **Body:** Peer support is open to Iowans navigating their own mental health recovery — whether that means coming out of a hard stretch, stepping down from a higher level of care, walking through a long-term condition, or just being tired of doing this alone. You don't need a specific diagnosis to call. You don't need to have tried everything else first. You don't need to be in crisis. And you don't need to know yet whether you want therapy, or peer support, or both — that's exactly the kind of thing the first call is for.

*(Rationale for including this section: the persona — and most peer-support seekers — assume they have to be "sick enough" or "recovered enough" to qualify, and self-disqualify before calling. A short, welcoming eligibility note removes that wall without making any pricing, insurance, or coverage claims. Per Justin's 2026-05-22 directive: no pricing or insurance-specific copy in this draft. Add funding language only when Justin decides the positioning.)*

## Per-service template variations

A few places this page diverges from the Community Support Program exemplar, and how I've handled them:

- **Stats row has zero numbers.** CSP's draft also avoided invented numbers, but used structural descriptors ("Ongoing," "In-home," "Des Moines metro") that were at least tied to scheduling or geography. Peer support is harder to summarize structurally — there's no fixed duration, no fixed setting, and we don't have caseload data yet. I leaned on the *three things peer support uniquely delivers* (lived experience, one-on-one relationship, pace-by-the-person) as the stats. If Justin provides real numbers (number of peer specialists on staff, typical engagement length, callers served per year), they should replace these.
- **"What is X?" section is longer than the CSP version.** This is intentional and load-bearing. Peer support is the least-understood service in Inside Out's catalog. The CSP page can assume the reader knows roughly what "case management" or "in-home support" means; this page cannot assume the reader knows what a peer specialist is. Three paragraphs instead of three sentences.
- **"What you can expect" Card 4 is emotional, not operational.** CSP's cards are all practical (visits, medications, paperwork, connections). For peer support, the fourth card has to land on the *hope* the relationship produces — because that's the actual deliverable that nothing else in Inside Out's catalog can promise. If the build wants four operational cards, the page loses what makes peer support different.
- **Step 2 of "How to get started" introduces a *matching* moment.** CSP just brings the worker; peer support has to acknowledge that the relationship only works if the lived experience overlaps in a way that lands. The "you can ask for a different one — that's allowed" line is small but important; it gives the persona permission they didn't know they had.
- **Eligibility section explicitly broadened to include non-crisis seekers.** Unlike CSP (oriented around chronic conditions) or mobile crisis (oriented around acute moments), peer support has to actively invite people who *aren't sure they qualify because nothing is actively wrong tonight.* That's most of the readers. The eligibility paragraph addresses them directly.
- **No "What to bring" / packing list.** Same as CSP — not applicable.
- **Crisis-line callout in hero CTA, not buried.** Same as every Inside Out service page, by site convention. The peer-support persona is non-acute, but readers in adjacent personas may be acute — the 844-428-3878 callout has to remain the primary CTA.

## Generalization check

Going section by section to confirm no copy is condition-specific, scenario-specific, or persona-specific:

- **Hero.** "Someone who's been where you are" — universal. "Has walked their own recovery and is still walking it" — true for any kind of mental health recovery, no diagnosis named. "Sit with what you're going through" — works for grief, long-term illness, post-crisis recovery, identity work, addiction recovery, anything. ✅
- **Stats row.** All three cards describe the *shape* of the service, not the user. ✅
- **What a peer specialist actually is.** I deliberately wrote *"a trained Iowan who has lived through their own mental health recovery"* — broad. I almost wrote "their own struggle with mental health" — broadened to "recovery" so it works for the persona who has stepped down out of stabilization, the persona who has lived with a condition for decades, and the persona who is just in a hard chapter. The paragraph about "for some people, peer support is the thing that finally breaks through... for others, it's what they reach for first" was written specifically to hold both the "tried therapy and it didn't take" persona AND the "haven't tried anything yet" persona on the same page without one feeling more central than the other. ✅
- **What you can expect — Card 2 (Talking about the real thing).** Originally said "the things you've never told your therapist" — broadened to "the things that feel too heavy to put on a therapist's desk" so it works for people who *have* a therapist and people who *don't*. ✅
- **What you can expect — Card 3 (Figuring out what comes next).** I named three sample scenarios — therapist search, return-to-work, defining recovery — to keep the card from feeling abstract. They were chosen to be additive (you can also be doing none of them) rather than gating. ✅
- **What we ask of you — Card 3 (Let this be a relationship, not a service).** This is the one card written for the persona specifically — the "tried therapy and it didn't take" persona is allergic to one-sided "expert tells you what to do" dynamics, and this card directly addresses that allergy. Re-read in adjacent personas: still works for someone newly out of stabilization (they need to know they have voice), still works for the family-pushed persona (it tells them they're the driver, not the family), still works for the long-term-recovery persona (it tells them peer support won't lecture them). ✅
- **How to get started.** Step 1 explicitly broadens the caller: *"You can be calling for yourself, or for someone you love."* Step 2's "you can ask for a different match" line works for any persona. Step 3's "as long as it's helping... we stop when you're ready" honors the autonomy that the persona — and every other reader — needs. ✅
- **Eligibility.** Explicitly opens the door for non-crisis seekers, post-crisis-stepdown seekers, long-term-condition seekers, and "tired of doing this alone" seekers in a single sentence. No diagnosis named. No tried-everything-first requirement. ✅
- **Tone overall.** Re-read in two other personas: (a) a 22-year-old who was recently discharged from inpatient stabilization, looking for steady company in the step-down — the page works, especially Steps 2 and 3 and the "earned hope" card; (b) a 47-year-old whose adult sibling is pushing them toward "doing something about" their recovery — the page works because the family-as-caller language in Step 1 is explicit, and the autonomy-preserving language in "What we ask of you" Card 3 mitigates the risk of the page reading as coercive. ✅

## Open questions for Justin

1. **Who are Inside Out's peer specialists, in practice?** How many CPS-credentialed peers are on staff? Are they full-time employees, part-time, contracted? Do they specialize (mental health vs. substance use recovery vs. youth)? Knowing this would let us put real numbers in the stats row and possibly a "Meet your peer team" section.
2. **Iowa CPS credential — confirm.** The draft asserts Inside Out's peers are Certified Peer Specialists per Iowa's credentialing pathway. Is that true for *every* peer on the team, or is some of the work done by uncredentialed peers under supervision? This affects how strongly we can lead with the credential.
3. **Youth peer support.** Iowa has a separate Youth CPS credential. Does Inside Out offer peer support to adolescents? Should the eligibility section explicitly include or exclude under-18s, or should youth peer support be a separate page entirely (parallel to crisis stabilization being split adult vs. children)?
4. **Substance use recovery peers.** Iowa also credentials recovery coaches / peer recovery specialists for substance use. Are Inside Out's peers cross-trained, or is substance-use peer support handled inside the Outpatient Substance Use Services line? The reader doesn't care about the org chart — the page just needs to know which door is the right one.
5. **Group vs. 1:1 vs. warm line.** The draft positions Inside Out peer support as a 1:1 relationship, distinct from NAMI-style groups and from anonymous warm lines. Confirm: is this accurate, or does Inside Out also run peer groups and/or a peer-staffed warm line? If yes to either, they each likely deserve their own section.
6. **Confidentiality boundaries.** The draft says peers "don't write notes that end up in a clinical chart." Is that strictly true (no documentation), or do peers document under a different system (progress notes, mandatory-reporting exceptions, billing requirements)? We need to be honest here without overpromising.
7. **Geographic coverage.** Is peer support offered in-person across all three Inside Out sites (Woodward / Perry / Knoxville-2026)? Available by phone or video for anyone in Iowa? Limited by service area? Vague here costs us calls.
8. **Caseload / capacity.** Any waiting time we should signal? "Typically matched within a week" or "matching may take longer in some service areas" — both are honest answers; neither is on the page yet.
9. **Funding / fees.** Per Justin's 2026-05-22 directive, no pricing copy in this draft. When ready, decide: is peer support Medicaid-billable? Free to the participant via grant funding? Mixed? The page currently is silent on cost. Reader will assume there *is* a cost unless told otherwise.
10. **Service-specific phone vs. crisis line.** 844-428-3878 is the primary CTA per Justin's directive. Is there a separate non-crisis peer-support intake number we should surface as a secondary, or is the 844 line the only door?
11. **Crossover with other Inside Out services.** Peer support is the service most likely to be the *wrong* first stop for someone who actually needs CSP, Mobile Crisis, or Stabilization — and vice versa. Should we add a "Looking for X? Try Y" callout at the bottom for the three most common mismatches, the way the CSP draft suggested?
12. **Naming the lived-experience areas without naming diagnoses.** Per directive, no diagnoses on the page. Are there *categories* of recovery experience Inside Out's peers are particularly equipped to walk with (e.g., "post-crisis stepdown," "long-term recovery," "first time reaching out," "co-occurring substance use") that we *could* name without naming a condition? Could strengthen the "Meet your peer" matching language in Step 2.

- [NAMI National — NAMI Connection](https://www.nami.org/support-education/support-groups/nami-connection/) — National parent of the Connection program. Source of the *"peer-led... led by people with mental health conditions"* language and the underlying philosophy of peer-led, structured, free support.
- [Iowa HHS — Iowa Peer Workforce Collaborative](https://hhs.iowa.gov/iowa-peer-workforce-collaborative) — Policy / workforce-development authority. Confirms the *Certified Peer Specialist (CPS)* credential exists in Iowa and underpins the legitimacy of calling Inside Out's peers "certified." Translate the policy voice; do not borrow it.
- [Optimae LifeServices](https://optimaelifeservices.com/) — Iowa peer / competitor. Useful as a counter-example: large Iowa provider with peer support inside a sprawling catalog and no clean public-facing page. Reinforces that the explainer gap is open for Inside Out to own.
- [Doors to Wellbeing — Copeland Center](https://www.doorstowellbeing.org/) — Outside-Iowa benchmark / SAMHSA-funded national TA center. Establishes peer support as an evidence-based, workforce-level discipline — not a volunteer add-on. *"Peer-led initiatives and training supporting the peer support workforce."*
- [Mental Health America — Peer Support](https://mhanational.org/peer-support) — Outside-Iowa benchmark. Cleanest one-sentence national definition of peer support: *"Peer Support refers to receiving help from individuals who have suffered from similar experiences."* Reference point for the explainer.
- [Fountain House](https://www.fountainhouse.org/) — Outside-Iowa benchmark / originator of the clubhouse model (NYC, founded 1948). Models the *"purpose, belonging, dignity"* trio and proves peer-driven recovery communities work at scale. We are not adopting the clubhouse model itself — we are borrowing the conviction that peers are the work, not the supporting cast.
- [International Association of Peer Supporters (iNAPS / APS)](https://www.peersupportworks.org/) — National practice-guidelines authority for peer support. Source for the standard that peer support is a credentialed discipline with national values (hope, mutuality, equality, self-determination). Used as reference; the site itself was intermittently reachable at fetch time.
